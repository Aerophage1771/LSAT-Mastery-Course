# Module 7: Evaluate the Argument -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 12 website lessons (7-1 through 7-11, plus 7-3a) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 7 is a well-structured unit with a clear pedagogical arc: introduction, method, target catalog, six drill lessons (each focused on a specific evaluation pattern), advanced concepts, and a reference guide. The module benefits from strong conceptual clarity -- the Yes/No Test is introduced early and reinforced consistently throughout. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **Only 3 of 12 lessons contain question-cards.** Lessons 7-3a (Missing Baseline), 7-4 (Unrepresentative Sample), and 7-5 (Rank vs. Raw Number) have real PT questions. The remaining nine lessons -- including the method lesson (7-2), three drill lessons (7-6, 7-7, 7-8), and both advanced lessons (7-9, 7-10) -- rely on illustrative blockquotes without interactive practice.
2. **Lessons 7-6, 7-7, and 7-8 are drills without actual questions.** They have "Practice Problem" headers and "Step-by-Step Analysis" sections but no question-card blocks with stimulus/options. The student is told to commit to an answer before scrolling, but no stimulus is present. These are incomplete.
3. **Lessons 7-1 and the diff alternate are heavily overlapping.** The diff version of 7-1 is 60 blocks -- nearly a full module overview -- duplicating content that 7-2 (method), 7-3 (targets), and the reference guide each cover individually.
4. **The reference guide (7-11) is the strongest in any module.** It includes the full method, Yes/No Test breakdown, all six target types, seven trap types, three difficulty factors, a rapid ID process, and a test-day checklist. It sets a high bar.
5. **No lesson covers the Prediction or Definition/Category targets with a worked example.** The catalog lesson (7-3) describes all six targets, but only four of the six get their own drill lesson. Predictions and Definitions are described in theory only.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 7-1: MODULE 7: Evaluate the Argument (57 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction and overview.

**Strengths:**
- Clear Question Goal statement up front -- the definition of Evaluate is precise and actionable
- The Strengthen/Weaken/Evaluate breakdown (bridge/widen/probe the gap) is excellent -- gives students a single framework that connects three question types
- The five question-stem patterns table is comprehensive and includes all real LSAT phrasings
- The "Distinguish from Strengthen/Weaken" callout addresses the most common student confusion
- The worked example (headache medication / placebo) is a strong teaching moment
- The "Why Wrong Answers Fail the Yes/No Test" callout clearly names the two failure modes

**Issues:**
- **Scope creep.** The lesson covers introduction, stem recognition, the Evaluate vs. Strengthen vs. Weaken comparison, the Neutral Evaluator Mindset, the Yes/No Test, common evaluation targets, AND a worked example. That is seven distinct teaching goals.
- **Overlap with 7-2 and 7-3.** The Yes/No Test is taught in full here AND in 7-2. The evaluation targets table appears here AND in 7-3. The mindset section is restated in 7-2 as the "Even If True" test.
- **No question-card.** At 57 blocks of theory, the student has no interactive practice.
- **The comparison table (Strengthen/Weaken/Evaluate)** is 5 rows and useful, but the "Number of Right Directions" row is potentially confusing -- "Two" could be misread as meaning two answers are correct.

**Suggested edits:**
- Cut the Yes/No Test section (it belongs in 7-2 as the method's Step 4). Replace with a brief forward reference.
- Cut the Common Evaluation Targets table (it belongs in 7-3). Replace with a one-sentence preview.
- Keep the comparison table, the stem patterns, and the Neutral Evaluator Mindset -- these are introduction-appropriate.
- Add a mini-exercise after the bridge example: "Which of these two questions would better evaluate the argument?"

### Lesson 7-2: The 4-Step Method (77 blocks)

**Role:** The complete repeatable method for Evaluate questions.

**Strengths:**
- Excellent structure: overview process block, then each step gets its own H2 with example, "When This Step Gets Tricky" callout, and concrete demonstration
- The "Even If True" Test for Step 2 is a powerful, memorable tool
- The Common Gap Patterns table (6 rows) is well-organized and covers all patterns
- The prephrase quality check (two properties: targets the gap + two-directional) is the single best test for a good prephrase
- The full worked example (speed cameras → hospital records system) walks through all 4 steps coherently
- The "When Prephrasing Gets Tricky" callout on multiple gaps is a valuable preview of advanced concepts

**Issues:**
- **No question-card.** The worked example is a blockquote + analysis, not an interactive question. The student cannot attempt the hospital records question before seeing the answer.
- **Step 1 (Find the Conclusion)** overlaps with general LR skills taught in Modules 1-2. Students arriving at Module 7 should already know how to find conclusions. Consider shortening this section.
- **The Yes/No Test appears in full here AND in 7-1.** The duplicate increases reading time without adding new insight.
- **Common Wrong Answer Patterns table (6 rows)** is thorough but overlaps with the reference guide (7-11). Consider whether this table belongs here or there.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a question-card after the Full Worked Example, giving the student one chance to apply the method.
- Shorten Step 1 to a brief reminder with a forward reference to Module 1.
- If 7-1 is trimmed of its Yes/No Test section, this lesson becomes the canonical location -- no overlap.

### Lesson 7-3: A Field Guide to Evaluation Targets (67 blocks)

**Role:** Catalog of all six evaluation targets with signal words, examples, and quick ID process.

**Strengths:**
- The Master Table (6 rows: type, frequency, core question, signal words) is excellent -- gives students a probability-based starting point
- Each target gets its own section with blockquote example, pivotal question pattern list, and Quick ID Process callout
- The Quick Identification Process (3-step process block) at the end ties everything together
- The frequency data (~30% causal, ~25% statistical, etc.) gives students a clear prioritization framework

**Issues:**
- **Overlap with 7-1.** The evaluation targets table appears in both lessons. If 7-1 is trimmed, this overlap resolves.
- **67 blocks is long for a catalog lesson.** The six targets plus the quick ID process could be split into two lessons (top 3 targets + bottom 3 targets) if length is a concern.
- **No question-card.** A catalog lesson does not strictly need one, but a "match the stimulus to the target type" exercise would reinforce the pattern recognition.
- **The Definitions/Categories target (~8%)** gets the least depth. The borderline-case concept is mentioned but not demonstrated with a Yes/No Test application.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a mini-exercise: "Read these three stimuli. Which evaluation target does each one represent?"
- Expand the Definitions/Categories section with a Yes/No Test demonstration -- this is the least intuitive target and needs more support.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add "Commit to your answer before scrolling" as a callout immediately after the question-card (it appears as paragraph text but could be more prominent).
- This lesson is the model for what 7-6, 7-7, and 7-8 should look like.

## Structural Issues

### 1. Six Drill Lessons, Only Three Have Question-Cards

Lessons 7-3a, 7-4, and 7-5 have real PT questions with interactive question-card blocks and full 5-option analyses. Lessons 7-6, 7-7, and 7-8 have "Practice Problem" headers and analysis sections but **no actual question-card blocks**. The stimuli are either missing entirely or embedded as blockquotes within the analysis breakdown, preventing interactive practice.

**Recommendation:** Add question-cards to 7-6, 7-7, and 7-8. These should be real PT questions where possible. This is the module's biggest content gap.

### 2. Advanced Lessons Reference Missing Questions

Lessons 7-9 and 7-10 both have "Practice Problem" sections that tell students to commit to an answer, followed by detailed analysis of specific answer choices. But the stimulus, question stem, and answer choices are not present. The student is asked to practice with content that does not exist.

**Recommendation:** Add the missing question-cards to both 7-9 and 7-10. The analyses are already written -- only the stimulus and options blocks need to be added.

### 3. Overlap Between 7-1 and Later Lessons

Lesson 7-1 (Introduction) teaches the Yes/No Test in full (also taught in 7-2), presents the evaluation targets table (also presented in 7-3), and includes a worked example with analysis. At 57 blocks, it functions as a condensed version of the entire module rather than a focused introduction.

**Recommendation:** Trim 7-1 to focus on three things: (a) What Evaluate questions are and how they relate to Strengthen/Weaken, (b) How to recognize the question stem, and (c) A brief preview of what the module covers. Move the Yes/No Test to 7-2 and the targets table to 7-3.

### 4. Two Evaluation Targets Have No Drill

Predictions (~10%) and Definitions/Categories (~8%) are described in the catalog (7-3) but have no dedicated drill lesson. The six drill lessons cover: Missing Baseline, Unrepresentative Sample, Rank vs. Raw Number, Causal Claims, Analogy, and Feasibility. This is reasonable given frequency -- Predictions and Definitions together account for only ~18% of questions. But students who encounter these rarer targets will have no practice.

**Recommendation:** Low priority. If the module expands, add a combined drill for Predictions + Definitions. Otherwise, acknowledge these gaps in the reference guide with brief worked examples.

## Summary of Priorities

1. **Add question-cards to lessons 7-6, 7-7, 7-8, 7-9, and 7-10** -- five lessons reference practice problems that do not exist. This is the module's most critical gap.
2. **Eliminate 7-1 overlap with 7-2 and 7-3** -- the introduction teaches content that later lessons own. Trim to introduction scope.
3. **Enrich the Reference Guide (7-11)** -- add the abstract language table and causal framework checklist from 7-9 and 7-2.
4. **Add "try it first" prompts before every worked analysis** -- small change, big pedagogical impact.
5. **Consider Prediction/Definition drills** -- the two rarest targets have no practice. Low priority but worth tracking.
