# Module 55: Advanced Passage 5 -- Psychology of Risk -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 6 website lessons (55-1 through 55-6) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 55 covers a single RC passage about loss aversion and risk-taking psychology, with one passage analysis lesson and five question breakdown lessons (Q22--Q26). The passage is well-chosen -- a social-science-meets-policy topic with a clear three-paragraph structure that tests multiple question types. The lesson quality is consistent but structurally thin. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **No "try it yourself" prompts before any worked analysis** -- all five question lessons jump directly from the question stem and answer choices to the full analysis. Students never get a moment to attempt the question independently before seeing the answer.
2. **The passage analysis (55-1) is solid but lacks a strategy layer** -- it breaks down each paragraph clearly but does not teach the student how to approach the passage structurally or what to anticipate from question stems.
3. **Good question-type variety** -- the five questions cover Application (Q22), Function (Q23), Author Viewpoint (Q24, Q26), and Structure/Organization (Q25). This is an excellent spread for a single passage.
4. **Minor typo in passage text** -- lessons 55-4, 55-5, and 55-6 contain "in fact." with an extraneous period ("in fact.,") in the passage accordion.
5. **No cross-question synthesis** -- the module lacks a wrap-up or strategy lesson that ties the five questions together and highlights what patterns emerged across the set.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 55-1: Passage Analysis (8 blocks)

**Role:** Passage presentation with paragraph-by-paragraph breakdown.

**Strengths:**
- The accordion passage is the full, unedited LSAT passage -- no truncation or summarization.
- Each paragraph gets its own H4 heading with a clear, plain-language summary.
- The paragraph nicknames ("The Main Idea," "Old Theory vs. New Research," "Applying It to Countries") are memorable and accurate.
- The Summary callout at the end is concise and captures the passage's argumentative arc.

**Issues:**
- **No approach tips.** The lesson breaks down what the passage says but does not teach the student what to look for or how to read strategically. For an "Advanced Passage" module, a section on reading strategy (e.g., "track the old theory vs. new research contrast") would be valuable.
- **No passage map.** A visual or tabular representation of the argument's structure (P1: phenomenon, P2: old vs. new, P3: application) would help students build a mental model before tackling questions.
- **The paragraph summaries use casual register** ("kicks off," "sure," "perfect example") which is effective for accessibility but could be paired with a more precise academic restatement for advanced students.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "Reading Strategy" callout before the paragraph breakdowns, noting the passage's contrast structure and the application-to-new-domain pattern.
- Add a short "What to Expect" section noting the types of questions this passage structure typically generates (function, application, inference).

### Lesson 55-2: Question 22 Breakdown (7 blocks)

**Role:** Application question -- applying passage concepts to a new scenario.

**Strengths:**
- Clear identification of the question type ("This is an Application question").
- The Prediction callout correctly identifies the scenario as a "gain" situation fitting the "previously accepted views."
- All five answer choices get thorough analysis with clear Correct/Incorrect badges.
- The wrong-answer explanations correctly distinguish between the old theory (rational risk for gain) and the new theory (loss aversion).

**Issues:**
- **No "try it yourself" prompt.** The analysis begins immediately after the answer choices. A brief prompt to attempt the question first would improve active learning.
- **The question block type is "blockquote" + "options"** rather than a question-card component. This means the student sees the question stem and choices but has no interactive attempt mechanism.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "Try this: Attempt the question before reading the analysis below" callout between the options block and the paragraph analysis.
- The answer-choice percentages (e.g., "[44.0%] (Correct)") are useful for seeing difficulty but also spoil the answer before the student can attempt it. Consider moving the "(Correct)" label out of the options list.

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Active Practice Moment in Any Question Lesson

All five question lessons (55-2 through 55-6) present the question and immediately provide the full analysis. There is no prompt asking the student to attempt the question before reading the answer. This is the module's biggest pedagogical gap.

**Recommendation:** Add a "Try this: Attempt the question before reading the analysis below" callout between the options block and the analysis paragraph in every question lesson.

### 2. Passage Analysis Lacks Strategy Content

Lesson 55-1 is a solid paragraph-by-paragraph breakdown but functions as a summary rather than a teaching tool. For an advanced module, it should also teach the student how to approach this *type* of passage (social science with theory contrast) and what question types to expect.

**Recommendation:** Add a "Reading Strategy" or "Approach Tips" section to 55-1 that covers the contrast structure and the application pattern.

### 3. No Module Wrap-Up or Synthesis

After completing all five questions, the student has no lesson that ties the experience together. A brief synthesis lesson could highlight: which question types appeared, which were hardest, and what passage-level strategies helped across the set.

**Recommendation:** Consider adding a 55-7 synthesis lesson or appending a "Module Takeaways" section to the final lesson (55-6).
