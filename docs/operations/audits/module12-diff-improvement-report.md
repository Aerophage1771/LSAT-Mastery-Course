# Module 12: Principle-Strengthen — Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 7 website lessons (12-1 through 12-7) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 12 is a well-constructed unit with a clear teaching arc: introduction to the concept → systematic method → four focused pattern drills → reference guide. The content is accurate, the worked examples are strong, and the wrong-answer analysis in the drill lessons is one of the best in the course library. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **Lesson 12-1 tries to do too much** — it covers introduction, what a principle is, three comparison tables (vs. Regular Strengthen, vs. Sufficient Assumption), five principle types, AND a worked example. This is a lot for a first lesson.
2. **No "try it first" prompts before any worked analysis** — all four drill lessons (12-3 through 12-6) present question-cards followed immediately by analysis, with no invitation for the student to attempt the problem first.
3. **Redundancy between Lesson 12-1 and Lesson 12-2** — the key "types of logical leaps" table and the pre-phrasing instructions appear in both lessons in nearly the same form.
4. **Redundancy between Lesson 12-1 and the Reference Guide (12-7)** — the five principle types table, the three-way comparison table (vs. Regular Strengthen, vs. Sufficient Assumption), and the abstraction guidance all appear in both 12-1 and 12-7 with minimal new depth added in the reference version.
5. **The Reference Guide (12-7) does not add a Definitional Principle drill** — despite naming it as one of five principle types throughout, no worked drill lesson covers it. The reference guide lists it in the table but there is no corresponding lesson.
6. **Lesson 12-2 lacks a worked example with a question-card** — it walks through a pharmaceutical example in blockquote/analysis format only; the student cannot attempt the question before reading the walkthrough.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 12-1: MODULE 12: Principle-Strengthen (Introduction)

**Role:** Module introduction and conceptual overview.

**Strengths:**
- The Question Goal statement at the top is excellent — precise and actionable.
- "Why This Matters" section successfully motivates the topic and cross-links to Flaw, Parallel Reasoning, and Sufficient Assumption.
- The frequency callout ("1–2 per scored section, medium-to-hard range") is appropriately scoped.
- The stem recognition table is comprehensive — six stem patterns with clear key signals.
- The "What Is a Principle?" section with the factory/river worked example is well-constructed and gives students a concrete mental model.
- The two comparison tables (vs. Regular Strengthen, vs. Sufficient Assumption) are genuinely useful and use the "analogy" framing (statute book vs. custom-fitted key) effectively.
- The five principle types table provides a clear taxonomy.
- The "Worked Example" (city mural project) is a well-chosen illustration.
- The Key Takeaways callout covers all six critical concepts cleanly.

**Issues:**
- **Scope creep.** The lesson covers: introduction + frequency, stem recognition, what a principle is, comparison to Regular Strengthen, comparison to Sufficient Assumption, five principle types, AND a worked example. That is seven distinct teaching goals.
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The worked example (city mural) is delivered entirely as a walkthrough. The student reads the argument, the four steps, and the conclusion — but is never invited to attempt the gap identification or pre-phrasing before the analysis begins.
- **The Worked Example (city mural) uses an invented argument** rather than a real PT stimulus. While illustrative, this understates how the skill transfers to actual test questions.
- **Five principle types are previewed but not yet practiced.** The lesson tells students about five types but then sends them to subsequent lessons for practice. This is fine structurally, but the preview competes for attention with the worked example.
- **The Principle-Strengthen vs. Sufficient Assumption table (blocks ~23-29)** says much of what the Reference Guide (12-7) says. A student who reads both will encounter the same comparison twice with nearly identical framing.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "try it first" prompt before the Worked Example section: "Before reading the walkthrough, take 90 seconds to identify the gap in the council member's argument and pre-phrase the principle you would need."
- Trim the lesson to its core teaching goals: introduction, stem recognition, what a principle is, and the worked example. Move the comparison tables (vs. Regular Strengthen, vs. Sufficient Assumption) to 12-2 or the Reference Guide.
- Replace the invented mural argument with a real PT stimulus, or at minimum flag it clearly as an illustrative (non-PT) example.

### Lesson 12-2: The 4-Step Method for Principle-Strengthen Questions

**Role:** The complete method for solving Principle-Strengthen questions.

**Strengths:**
- The 4-step method structure is clear and well-organized: Find Conclusion → Identify Leap → Abstract Principle → Match Answer.
- The timing callout (45 seconds for Steps 1–3, 35 seconds for Step 4) is practical and specific.
- The logical leap classification table (Fact → Value, Means → End, Role → Duty, Features → Category, Comparison → Choice) is excellent — gives students a vocabulary for naming the gap before pre-phrasing.
- Pre-phrase templates by leap type are actionable and correct.
- The Abstraction Check callout ("Would this rule apply to cases other than the one in the stimulus?") is the best single piece of advice in the module.
- The wrong answer patterns table (Contradiction, Scope Shift, Premise Restatement, Mistaken Reversal, Partial Justification) is comprehensive and well-described.
- The pharmaceutical company worked example is substantive and well-chosen.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The pharmaceutical worked example is the only hands-on content in this lesson, but it is presented entirely as a pre-analyzed walkthrough. The student reads the argument and the analysis simultaneously rather than having a chance to apply the method first.
- **The worked example does not include a question-card.** The analysis is well-written, but without an actual set of answer choices, the student cannot practice Step 4 (evaluating and eliminating choices). The lesson teaches the method but provides no opportunity to use it against real answer choices.
- **Overlap with 12-1.** Step 2 (Identify the Logical Leap) introduces the same domain-shift taxonomy (Fact → Value, etc.) that is also covered in 12-1's worked example. Step 3 (Pre-phrasing) walks through the same two-part abstraction method that 12-1 implies. A student reading both lessons encounters the same frameworks twice.
- **The wrong answer patterns table is strong**, but it duplicates nearly all of the "Common Traps" section in the Reference Guide (12-7). Consider whether one location should own this content.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "try it first" prompt before the pharmaceutical worked example: "Attempt Steps 1–3 on your own before reading the walkthrough. Identify the conclusion, the leap, and pre-phrase the principle."
- Add a brief set of illustrative answer choices to the pharmaceutical worked example so the student can practice Step 4.
- Consider trimming the intro to Step 2 by referencing the leap types introduced in 12-1 rather than re-presenting the full table.

### Lesson 12-3: The Normative Bridge (Drill)

**Role:** Focused drill on the Normative Bridge principle pattern.

**Strengths:**
- Clear conceptual opening: the Hume "is-ought gap" framing is accurate, engaging, and memorable.
- The is-ought template callout is well-structured and immediately applicable.
- The common variations table (5 rows covering harm → moral wrongness, negative effect → policy rejection, etc.) gives students pattern recognition before they see the question.
- Real PT question: PT-117-S-4-Q-2. The pacifist/self-defense stimulus is a strong test of this specific principle type.
- The breakdown of the argument (three quoted sections with analysis) is excellent — it models careful stimulus dissection.
- Full wrong-answer analysis for all five choices, with specific badges and clear reasoning for each.
- The visual summary table at the end ("Does It Bridge Act → Threat?") is a useful review.
- Key Takeaways cover all five critical insights from this drill.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The question-card appears immediately followed by the Full Analysis header with no pause. The student moves directly from seeing the question to reading the analysis without being invited to attempt the problem.
- **The wrong answer analysis for (A)** is accurate but could note the "wrong logical direction" issue more sharply — (A) muddies the argument's absolute structure, which is the deeper problem, not just "irrelevant distinction."
- **The conceptual classification callout** ("This is a Normative Bridge gap, but of a specific subtype: value-to-value") is a useful addition, but it deserves slightly more emphasis. Students may not notice the subtype distinction on first read.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "try it first" prompt immediately after the question-card and before the Full Analysis header.
- Consider bolding or visually separating the "subtype" insight in the Classifying the Gap callout.

### Lesson 12-4: The Efficiency Principle (Drill)

**Role:** Focused drill on the Efficiency Principle pattern.

- The common variations table covers four realistic patterns (expensive = cheap in results; complex has no advantage; etc.).
- Real PT question: PT-112-S-1-Q-15. The educational tools study question is a strong test of the equal-outcomes + cost comparison structure.
- The "no worse than" / "no better than" language observation is an important diagnostic that would be easy to miss without explicit mention.
- Strong wrong-answer analysis for all five choices.
- The visual summary table is effective.
- Key Takeaways close with five useful insights including the note about conditional language ("should not... unless").

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** Like the other drill lessons, the question-card is followed immediately by the analysis.
- **There is a horizontal rule between the "The Problem" header and the question-card** that appears in the middle of the lesson structure — a minor formatting inconsistency (the other drills do not have this).
- **The wrong-answer analysis for (B)** identifies the answer as a "scope mismatch" but the deeper problem is that it mischaracterizes the evidence as showing ineffectiveness rather than equal effectiveness. The current explanation mentions this but could be cleaner.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "try it first" prompt after the question-card.
- Remove the extra horizontal rule between "The Problem" and the question-card.

**Suggested edits:**

### Lesson 12-6: The Lesser of Two Evils (Drill)

**Role:** Focused drill on the Lesser-of-Two-Evils principle pattern.

**Strengths:**
- The "acknowledged trade-offs" framing is accurate and differentiates this type clearly from the other four.
- The "hierarchy of errors" concept is a strong contribution — it gives students a label for the abstract principle type they are looking for.
- The diagnostic checklist (concession + two flawed systems + hardline conclusion) is the most operationally useful pattern-recognition tool in any of the drills.
- The Lesser-of-Two-Evils template callout is well-structured.
- Real PT question: PT-113-S-2-Q-22. The political theorist/sentencing question is an ideal test of this pattern.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** Same issue across all drill lessons.

- **Answer (E) analysis** correctly identifies the "extreme language" trap, but does not note that this is a variant of the Scope Mismatch trap (the principle addresses a different severity level than the argument uses). **Suggested edits:**

## Structural Issues

### 1. Missing Definitional Principle Drill

The module names five principle types — Normative Bridge, Efficiency, Accountability, Lesser-of-Two-Evils, and Definitional — but only four are drilled. There is no lesson for the Definitional Principle. Every lesson that lists the five types points students toward "the lessons that follow" but no such lesson exists for Definitional.

**Recommendation:** Add a Lesson 12-5b or extend the module by one lesson covering the Definitional Principle with a real PT question.

### 2. Universal Absence of "Try It First" Prompts

All four drill lessons (12-3 through 12-6) and the method lesson (12-2) present question-cards or worked examples with no pause between the stimulus and the analysis. The student reads the question and the answer simultaneously. This is the single highest-priority pedagogical fix.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief "Try this before reading on" prompt immediately after each question-card and before each analysis section. A single sentence is sufficient.

### 3. Lesson 12-1 and the Reference Guide (12-7) Duplicate Each Other

The five principle types table, the three-way comparison table, and the abstraction guidance appear in both 12-1 and 12-7 in nearly identical form. This is the module's biggest structural redundancy.

**Recommendation:** Lesson 12-1 should introduce the concepts and point to 12-7 for the definitive reference. The Reference Guide should add new organizational content (timing tables, quick-check exercises) rather than repeat 12-1's teaching material.

### 4. Lesson 12-2 Has No Interactive Practice

The method lesson is the most important lesson in the module, but it provides no opportunity for the student to attempt anything. The pharmaceutical worked example is delivered entirely in "here's how it's done" format.

**Recommendation:** Add a question-card to 12-2 so students can apply the 4-step method independently. Even a medium-difficulty Principle-Strengthen question with full answer choices would close this gap.
