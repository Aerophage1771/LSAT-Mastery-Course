# Module 26: Analogy -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 2 website lessons (26-1 through 26-2) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 26 covers Analogy questions in two lessons: Introduction (26-1) and Step-by-Step Approach (26-2). This is one of the smaller modules, matching its relatively low frequency (~4% of RC questions). The content is conceptually strong with a clear teaching model (abstract the principle, then match), but the module suffers from the same practice gap as other RC modules. Key improvement opportunities:

1. **No practice questions.** Neither lesson includes a question-card or worked example. The abstract-then-match method is described but never demonstrated.
2. **Lesson 26-1 has redundancy between the opening and stem identification.** The "What's this question asking?" section and the Stem Identification section restate the same core concept (ignore surface topics, match abstract structure).
3. **The tie-breaker strategies in 26-2 are strong.** The Reverse Test and Focus on the Difference are genuinely useful heuristics that could be highlighted more prominently.
4. **Missing: common trap answer patterns.** Unlike modules 24 and 25, this module has no trap/distractor lesson. Even a brief section on common wrong-answer patterns for analogy questions would be valuable.

---

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 26-1: Introduction (11 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction, stem identification, and importance statement.

**Strengths:**
- The three-step framework in the opening (Identify the original relationship, State it abstractly, Test the choices) is the lesson's strongest teaching moment. It gives students a concrete procedure.
- Three stem categories (Direct Comparison, Relational Parallel, Exemplification) with real examples cover the full range of analogy stems.
- The Overarching Takeaway callout correctly emphasizes that topic-matching is the main trap.
- The importance section provides helpful strategic framing: analogy skills are transferable to LR Parallel Reasoning.

**Issues:**
- **Redundancy.** The opening section ("What's this question asking?") and the Stem Identification section's introductory paragraph both define what analogy questions do. The stem section's intro paragraph could be cut.
- **The importance section hedges too much.** It tells students not to let analogy questions "become a major stumbling block" -- which is good strategic advice -- but the tone could be more encouraging. At ~4% of questions, this is a low-frequency type, but students who master it can reliably add points.
- **No practice question.** A mini-exercise asking students to abstract a relationship would reinforce the three-step framework.

**Suggested edits:**
- Cut the stem identification section's introductory paragraph (redundant with opening).
- Add a mini-exercise after the three-step framework: "Abstract this scenario into a general principle."

---

### Lesson 26-2: Step-by-Step Approach (8 blocks)

**Role:** The 2-step method plus two tie-breaker strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The 2-step method (Isolate & Abstract, Test Each Choice) is clean and correctly focused on the core skill.
- The worked example in Step 1 (scientific tool becoming a children's toy) is concrete and well-chosen.
- Tie-Breaker #1 (The Reverse Test) is an excellent heuristic -- checking if the answer and the original are interchangeable blueprints.
- Tie-Breaker #2 (Focus on the Difference) is practical and addresses the common "stuck between two" scenario.

**Issues:**
- **No worked example of the full method.** Step 1 has a brief abstract-rule example, but the method is never demonstrated end-to-end on a real or simulated question.
- **No question-card.** The student has no opportunity to practice.
- **Step 2 is described generally.** "Be ruthless -- eliminate any choice that has extra components, missing pieces, or a different sequence" is good advice but lacks a concrete illustration.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a full worked example demonstrating both steps on a sample question.
- Add a question-card after the worked example.
- Provide one concrete elimination example in Step 2.

---

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Practice Questions

Both lessons are theory-only. The abstract-then-match skill is inherently hands-on and benefits enormously from practice.

**Recommendation:** Add at least one question-card, ideally in Lesson 26-2 after the method description.

### 2. No Trap/Distractor Lesson

Unlike modules 24 and 25, this module has no lesson dedicated to common wrong-answer patterns. For analogy questions, common traps include: topic-matching (same subject, wrong structure), partial-match (gets some components right but not all), and reversed-direction (correct components but wrong causal direction).

**Recommendation:** Add a brief traps section to Lesson 26-2, or create a short Lesson 26-3 covering common analogy distractors.
