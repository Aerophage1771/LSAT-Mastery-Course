# Module 43: Practice Passage 2 -- Bankruptcy Laws (PT-128-S-1-P-2) -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 9 website lessons (43-1 through 43-9) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 43 covers a persuasive/argumentative passage about the evolution of bankruptcy law from punitive to remedial models. The 9 lessons comprise a passage analysis (43-1) followed by 8 individual question breakdowns (43-2 through 43-9). This is the strongest practice passage module of the four reviewed, with exceptionally well-structured analyses, a good mix of question types, and consistently accurate LSAT reasoning. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **No "try it yourself" prompt before any question analysis** -- Same gap as Module 42. All 8 question lessons launch directly into analysis without encouraging independent attempt.
2. **The passage analysis (43-1) is excellent but purely lecture** -- It provides a strong paragraph-by-paragraph breakdown and an effective summary, but includes no active learning exercise.
3. **The question mix is the most diverse of the four modules** -- Covers main point, inference/implication, attitude, primary purpose, perspective-taking, completion, suggestion, and weaken. This diversity is a strength that should be explicitly called out.
4. **No question-type tags** -- As with Module 42, no lessons explicitly identify the question type or provide a transferable approach tip.
5. **The weaken question (43-9) is the most pedagogically rich** -- Its analysis clearly identifies the author's core premise and shows how to find evidence that attacks it. This lesson could serve as a model for how to approach weaken questions.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 43-1: Passage Analysis

**Role:** Initial passage breakdown and reading comprehension guide.

**Strengths:**
- Clear three-paragraph analysis correctly identifying: (1) thesis and central conflict, (2) the old system's failures, (3) the modern system's mechanics and goals.
- The summary callout effectively distills the passage into three bullet points: central argument, flaws of the past, strengths of the present.
- Correctly identifies the passage as a persuasive argument (not neutral exposition), which is critical for answering attitude and purpose questions later.

**Issues:**
- **No active learning exercise.** The lesson is all lecture. A brief prompt like "Before reading on, can you identify the author's thesis and their strongest piece of evidence?" would improve engagement.
- **No forward-looking question preview.**

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a question-preview section noting the 8 questions cover main point, inference, attitude, purpose, perspective-taking, completion, suggestion, and weaken types.
- Add a mini-exercise after the paragraph 1 analysis asking the student to identify the thesis.

## Structural Issues

### 1. Missing "Try It Yourself" Prompts Across All Question Lessons

Same critical gap as Module 42. All 8 question lessons jump directly to analysis.

### 2. No Question-Type Identification

None of the 8 question lessons tag their question type. This module has the most diverse question-type distribution of the four reviewed, making type-tagging especially valuable.

### 3. No Cross-Question Synthesis

No wrap-up lesson synthesizes the question types, key traps, or passage-specific skills. Given that this passage has 8 questions (the most of the four modules), a synthesis would be especially useful.
