# Module 32: Third-Party Viewpoint -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 2 website lessons (32-1 through 32-2) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 32 covers Third-Party Viewpoint questions (identifying the beliefs or arguments of a specific non-author person or group mentioned in the passage) in two lessons: Introduction (32-1) and Step-by-Step Approach (32-2). At ~5% of RC questions (108 appearances), this is a moderately common type. The module is well-structured with a clear attribution-tracking methodology. Key improvement opportunities:

1. **No practice questions.** Neither lesson includes a question-card or worked example.
2. **The 4-step method in 32-2 is the module's best content** -- the progression from identification to isolation to paraphrase to matching is pedagogically sound.
3. **The module should explicitly connect to Module 30 (Author Viewpoint).** The "Check Ownership" skill in Module 30 is the flip side of the attribution skill taught here. A cross-reference would help students see the connection.
4. **The single stem category in 32-1 is too narrow.** Third-party viewpoint questions can also ask about what the third party would predict, endorse, or reject -- not just what they believe.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 32-1: Introduction (10 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction, stem identification, and importance statement.

**Strengths:**
- Clear opening with a four-step detection framework (Locate, Identify attributed claims, Distinguish viewpoints, Check evidential backing).
- The emphasis on attribution language ("according to," "claims," "argues," "suggests") is precise and actionable.
- The Overarching Takeaway correctly frames the task as "act as a neutral reporter."
- Frequency data (108 times, ~5%) properly positions this as a moderately common type.
- The importance section correctly identifies the connection to comparative passages and the main trap (confusing third-party with author views).

**Issues:**
- **Only one stem category ("Specific Individuals or Groups").** This is accurate but incomplete. Third-party viewpoint questions can also ask for predictions ("What would the scientist most likely predict?"), endorsements ("Which approach would the critics most likely endorse?"), or disagreements ("The two researchers would most likely disagree about..."). These variant forms deserve their own categories.
- **Redundancy.** The opening four-step framework and the stem identification section both cover attribution tracking. The stem section's intro paragraph restates the opening.
- **No practice question.**

**Suggested edits:**
- Add 2-3 more stem categories (Prediction, Endorsement/Rejection, Comparative Third-Party Disagreement).
- Cut the redundant intro paragraph from the stem section.
- Add a mini-exercise.

---

### Lesson 32-2: Step-by-Step Approach (10 blocks)

**Role:** The 4-step method plus two tie-breaker strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The 4-step method (Identify the Party, Locate Attributions, Isolate & Paraphrase, Match to Answers) is clean, logical, and well-sequenced.
- Step 2's attribution phrase examples ("Smith argues that...", "According to the critics...", "Proponents of this view claim...") give students concrete search targets.
- Step 3's instruction to "ignore what the author thinks about their view" is critical -- it addresses the single most common error on this question type.
- Tie-Breaker #1 (Strict Attribution Check) directly addresses author-vs-third-party confusion.
- Tie-Breaker #2 ("Who Said What?" Test) addresses multi-party misattribution.

**Issues:**
- **No worked example.** The attribution-tracking method is inherently passage-dependent and would benefit enormously from a concrete demonstration.
- **No question-card.**
- **No connection to Module 30.** The "Check Ownership" skill taught in Module 30 is the same skill viewed from the opposite direction. A cross-reference would help.
- **Step 3's "ignore what the author thinks" instruction could be expanded** with an example showing how author commentary around a third-party claim can mislead.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a worked example showing the 4-step method applied to a multi-perspective passage excerpt.
- Add a question-card.
- Add a cross-reference to Module 30's "Check Ownership" point.
- Expand Step 3 with a brief example of author commentary that could mislead.

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Practice Questions

Attribution tracking is a reading-dependent skill that benefits from practice with real passage text. A worked example demonstrating the difference between author commentary and third-party claims would be the single highest-impact addition.

**Recommendation:** Add one question-card to Lesson 32-2.

### 2. Narrow Stem Categories

The single stem category in 32-1 covers the core phrasing but misses variant forms (prediction, endorsement, comparative disagreement). Expanding the stem categories would better prepare students for the full range of questions.

**Recommendation:** Add 2-3 stem categories to Lesson 32-1.

### 3. No Cross-Module Connection

Modules 30 and 32 teach complementary skills (author ownership checking and third-party attribution tracking). Cross-referencing them would help students build a unified perspective-tracking skill.

**Recommendation:** Add cross-references in both modules.
