# Module 13: Principle-Apply — Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 7 website lessons (13-1 through 13-7) evaluated as a standalone content unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 13 is a well-structured and high-quality unit. The concept work in lessons 13-1 and 13-2 is clear and precise, the three drill lessons each feature a real PT question with full wrong-answer analysis, and the reference guide (13-7) is unusually complete. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **No "try it first" prompts before any worked analysis.** All three drill lessons (13-3, 13-4, 13-5, 13-6) present the question-card and then immediately launch into step-by-step analysis. There is no pause inviting the student to attempt the question independently before reading the solution. This is the module's most consistent and highest-priority gap.
2. **Lesson 13-1 (Introduction) duplicates significant content from 13-2 (Step-by-Step Guide).** Both lessons teach the decomposition process, the Principle-Apply vs. Principle-Strengthen contrast, and common principle structures. A student who reads both in sequence encounters the same ideas twice without meaningful new depth.
3. **The worked example in 13-1 and 13-2 is illustrative only — no real stimulus or question-card.** The government-regulation example in 13-1 and the travel-reimbursement example in 13-2 describe what the analysis would look like but don't present actual answer choices for the student to evaluate. This is a missed opportunity for active practice in the first two lessons.
4. **The drill lessons (13-3 through 13-6) are the module's strongest content.** Each follows the same four-step structure, includes a real PT question-card with full wrong-answer breakdown, and closes with a synthesis callout. The addition of "try it first" prompts would make these lessons near-perfect.
5. **The reference guide (13-7) is the best reference guide in the curriculum.** It covers all five application types, the full three-way comparison table (Apply vs. Strengthen vs. Generalization), seven named trap patterns, and an 8-item test-day checklist. It is genuinely comprehensive — unlike reference guides in some other modules that are thin summaries.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 13-1: Introduction (approx. 45 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction — explains what Principle-Apply is, how to recognize it, and how the matching process works.

**Strengths:**
- The Question Goal statement is clear and precise.
- The "Why This Matters" section effectively motivates the topic with everyday analogies and explains the cross-question-type payoff.
- The Frequency & Difficulty callout gives accurate context (1–2 per section, full difficulty range).
- The recognition table (5 stem patterns) is accurate and practical.
- The Principle-Apply vs. Principle-Strengthen comparison table is the most useful single artifact in the lesson — the six-row contrast clearly distinguishes the two question types.
- The "Direction Test" callout is memorable and actionable.
- The Common Principle Structures table (conditional rule, definitional criterion, ethical guideline, causal principle) is a strong pedagogical anchor.

**Issues:**
- **Redundancy with Lesson 13-2.** The decomposition process ("Break the Principle into Conditions and Consequences"), the "All Conditions Rule" callout, and the Worked Example section in 13-1 cover nearly the same ground as Steps 1–2 and the Full Worked Example in 13-2. A student who reads both lessons encounters parallel decomposition examples in back-to-back lessons without meaningful new content.
- **Worked Example is illustrative, not interactive.** The government-regulation example presents the principle and a conceptual description of what the analysis would show — but there are no actual answer choices for the student to evaluate. This is an explanation masquerading as practice.
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The walkthrough section in the Worked Example proceeds directly to analysis without asking the student to attempt anything first.
- **Lesson length vs. scope.** At ~45 blocks, this lesson tries to introduce the question type, teach the matching process, and walk through a worked example. This is more than an introduction needs to do. The matching process and worked example could move to Lesson 13-2, allowing 13-1 to focus purely on recognition and framing.

**Suggested edits:**
- Cut or drastically trim the Matching Process section (Steps 1–2 with the defective-product breakdown). Let Lesson 13-2 own that content completely.
- If the Worked Example stays, replace the illustrative description with a real stimulus and actual answer choices so the student has something concrete to attempt.
- Add a forward reference at the end: "You will practice the step-by-step method in Lesson 2, and drill all three application patterns in Lessons 3–6."

### Lesson 13-2: The 4-Step Method (approx. 38 blocks)

**Role:** Systematic method for solving Principle-Apply questions — the operational core of the module.

**Strengths:**
- Excellent structure: the 4-Step overview, then each step gets a full section with example and callout.
- The Conditional Diagramming table (6 rows of logical connectives) is clear, accurate, and practical.
- The Hidden Conditions callout is valuable — it names the category of subtle modifiers ("knowingly," "without provocation," etc.) that students routinely miss.
- The five-trap table (Missing Condition, Wrong Consequence, Mistaken Reversal, Scope Mismatch, Irrelevant Criterion) is well-named and well-explained.
- The Verification Habit callout is motivationally effective.
- The Timing callout (1:30 per question) is a useful anchor.

**Issues:**
- **Full Worked Example is illustrative only.** The travel-reimbursement example describes what the 4-step walkthrough would produce but doesn't present answer choices for the student to evaluate before the analysis. The student reads a worked example without doing any work.
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The walkthrough proceeds directly to analysis without asking the student to attempt the decomposition or evaluate any answer options first.
- **Overlap with 13-1.** Both lessons teach multi-condition decomposition using similar examples (defective product in 13-1; transit-eligibility in 13-2). The examples are different but the teaching point is identical. If 13-1 is trimmed, this overlap resolves.
- **The four-trap table in Step 3 is strong but doesn't include Excluded-Category or Overly Broad Interpretation traps.** Those are taught in 13-5 and 13-6 respectively and appear in the reference guide — but the Step 3 section doesn't acknowledge them, leaving an implicit gap.

**Suggested edits:**
- Replace the illustrative travel-reimbursement walkthrough with a real question-card so the student can attempt the problem before reading the 4-step analysis.
- Add a brief "Try this before reading on" prompt before the walkthrough section.
- Add a forward reference at the end of the trap table: "Two additional trap types — Excluded-Category and Overly Broad Interpretation — appear in Lessons 5 and 6."

### Lesson 13-3: Drill — Direct Application (approx. 46 blocks) [PT-104-S-4-Q-1]

**Role:** First drill lesson — direct application pattern, government taxation question.

**Strengths:**
- The conceptual introduction to "direct application" is clear and accurate.
- The distinction between direct and contrapositive application is correctly drawn.
- The checklist decomposition is excellent: the Hidden Condition callout correctly identifies the consequence's embedded requirement (the tax must be *for the purpose of* discouraging use, not just any tax).
- Five-option breakdown is thorough and accurate — each wrong answer gets a specific verdict label (Wrong Goal, Contradicts Consequence, Wrong Action, Wrong Direction).
- The visual summary table is a strong synthesis artifact.
- The five Key Takeaways are well-chosen and non-redundant.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The question-card is presented and then immediately followed by the full analysis without any invitation to attempt the problem first. This is the most important fix for this lesson.
- The opening two paragraphs defining "direct application" are slightly repetitive — the first paragraph ends with "the LSAT constructs wrong answers that match the principle on every dimension except one" and the second paragraph immediately restates this with "the LSAT doesn't make it easy by having wrong answers fail on obvious conditions."

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "Try this:" prompt immediately after the "Instructions" paragraph and before the question-card display: "Attempt the question before reading the analysis below."
- Tighten the opening two paragraphs into one — the restated point about subtle wrong answers can be cut.

### Lesson 13-4: Drill — Contrapositive Application (approx. 40 blocks) [PT-106-S-1-Q-10]

**Role:** Second drill lesson — contrapositive application pattern, economic growth question.

**Strengths:**
- The conceptual introduction to the contrapositive is precise and accurate.
- The "Spotting Contrapositive Questions" callout (with bullet list of signal phrases: "only when," "requires," "cannot... unless") is practical and actionable.
- The two-row breakdown (Original vs. Contrapositive) makes the logical transformation transparent.
- The five-option analysis is excellent — each answer gets a specific error label (Reversal Trap, Overly Broad, Contradiction, Correct, Distortion + Reversal).
- The pattern observation ("four of five wrong answers claim economic growth WILL occur") is a powerful insight that generalizes beyond this question.
- The five Key Takeaways are specific and actionable.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The question-card is again presented without any invitation to attempt it before reading the analysis. Same issue as 13-3.
- The question-card appears *after* a `<br/><br/><br/>` spacer and an `<hr/>` — but in the lesson as currently written, the question-card appears *before* the "Full Analysis" heading, not after it. This means the question-card serves as the problem but there's no explicit signal telling the student they should now attempt it.
- The opening section correctly introduces the contrapositive but doesn't explicitly state this is a drill with a real PT question until the "Drill" subheading. A student skimming might miss the transition.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add "**Try this:** Attempt the question before reading the analysis below." immediately after the question-card in the problem section.
- Optionally promote the drill structure more explicitly: add a heading like "Your Turn" between the question-card and the Full Analysis heading.

### Lesson 13-5: Drill — Definitional Checklist (approx. 42 blocks) [PT-123-S-2-Q-7]

**Role:** Third drill lesson — definitional checklist pattern, moral motivation question.

**Strengths:**
- The Definitional Checklist Method (3-step process block) is clear and well-structured.
- The "Exclusion Test Is Usually Decisive" callout is the most actionable insight in the lesson — it gives students a fast elimination heuristic.
- The three-item checklist (positive criterion + two exclusion criteria) is perfectly constructed for the question.
- The five-option categorization is thorough — each answer is sorted into a named bin (Societal Norms, Self-Interest, Advanced Motivation), and the rationale is precise.
- The observation about Jadine's use of "always" as a universalizing marker is a genuinely useful teaching point.
- The visual summary table with category bins is the clearest summary table in the module.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** Same issue as 13-3 and 13-4 — the question-card is presented without an invitation to attempt it first.
- The opening contrast between definitional and conditional Principle-Apply is slightly oversimplified: definitional questions are presented as requiring "categorization, not conditional logic." In practice, the categorization itself is a form of conditional matching (does this person's motivation satisfy the positive criterion AND fail both exclusion criteria?). The distinction is useful pedagogically but could be stated more carefully.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "Try this:" prompt immediately before the Full Analysis section.
- Optionally add a note that the three-item checklist (positive + exclusion criteria) is itself a conditional structure: "IF abstract principle (not self-interest and not societal norms) → advanced motivation."

### Lesson 13-6: Drill — Advanced Application (approx. 45 blocks) [PT-119-S-2-Q-21]

**Role:** Fourth drill lesson — multi-condition/high-threshold pattern, duty-fulfillment question.

**Strengths:**
- The "Anatomy of an Advanced Principle" breakdown (four feature types: multi-condition, high-threshold language, nested conditionals, abstract ethical rules) is pedagogically excellent — it names the categories of difficulty that make advanced questions hard.
- The Threshold Matching Strategy callout (two-column mental ledger) is a practical metacognitive tool.
- The two-item threshold breakdown (overwhelming evidence calibration vs. disastrous consequences calibration) is the clearest threshold-teaching content in any LR module.
- The five-option analysis is rigorous — the identification of choice (E) as the "hardest trap" because it meets one threshold but fails the other is the most important insight in the module for advanced test-takers.
- The "fulfill the duty is the default" principle is a correct generalization that helps students approach this entire question type.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** Same issue as all other drill lessons.
- The question-card appears after the principle is discussed at length — by the time the student sees the options, they've already been primed with significant analysis. The prompt structure slightly undermines independent practice.
- The "Competing Duties" point (scope mismatch in choice D) is correct but could be expanded — this is a genuine category of difficulty that appears repeatedly on advanced Principle-Apply questions, and a single sentence in the breakdown may not be enough to generalize it.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "Try this:" prompt immediately after the question-card display and before the "Full Analysis" heading.
- Consider expanding the scope-mismatch section (choice D analysis) to flag "competing duties as an out-of-scope scenario" as a named pattern, parallel to the numbered trap types in 13-7.

### Lesson 13-7: Reference Guide (approx. 55 blocks)

**Role:** Complete one-stop reference for the Principle-Apply module.

**Strengths:**
- The reference guide is genuinely comprehensive — the most complete reference guide in the curriculum. It covers the 4-Step Method, Bridge Diagnostics table, Application Types table (all five types), three-way comparison table (Apply vs. Strengthen vs. Generalization), Trigger-Checking Protocol (6-step process), seven named trap patterns with examples, and an 8-item test-day checklist.
- The three-way comparison table is outstanding — it compares Principle-Apply, Principle-Strengthen, and Principle-Generalization across 9 features. This is the clearest disambiguation of these three related question types in any module.
- The seven trap patterns (Sufficient-Necessary Reversal, Condition Distortion, Wrong Conclusion, High-Threshold Trap, Scope Mismatch, Excluded-Category Trap, Overly Broad Interpretation) are all named, described, and illustrated with examples.
- The Bridge Diagnostics table is unique to this reference guide and provides a fast-scan protocol absent from other modules.

**Issues:**
- **The website version uses "conditions" and "consequence" vocabulary** while the mastery course uses "trigger" and "verdict." Within the website lesson, the reference guide uses "conditions" terminology from the website, which is slightly inconsistent with the diagnostic vocabulary introduced in other parts of the reference guide (which uses "trigger" in its Bridge Diagnostics table). The mastery course resolves this cleanly.
- **The Application Types table in the reference guide** (5 types) covers content not explicitly drilled in the lesson sequence. "Ethical / Abstract Application" and "Multi-Condition / High-Threshold" are named types in the table, but only Multi-Condition gets its own drill (13-6). The reference guide thus covers types the student hasn't explicitly practiced.
- **No visual quick-reference.** For a test-day reference guide, a compact visual summary (one-page card format) would be more useful than the current layout, which presents full prose descriptions even in the tables.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a brief note at the Application Types table explaining which drill covers which type: "Direct Application → Lesson 3, Contrapositive → Lesson 4, Definitional → Lesson 5, Advanced/High-Threshold → Lesson 6."
- Minor: reconcile "conditions/consequence" vs. "trigger/verdict" vocabulary within the reference guide to one consistent set.
- Consider adding a compact "Quick Reference Card" callout at the top summarizing the 4-step method in 4 bullet points — useful for skimming on test day.

## Structural Issues

### 1. No "Try It First" Prompts in Any Drill Lesson

All four drill lessons (13-3 through 13-6) present the question-card without any prompt inviting independent attempt. The student is immediately given the full analysis context (principle diagram, checklist, step-by-step walkthrough). This is the module's highest-priority structural gap.

**Recommendation:** Add "**Try this:** Attempt the question before reading the analysis below." immediately after the question-card display in each drill lesson, before the "Full Analysis" heading or equivalent.

### 2. Conceptual Redundancy Between 13-1 and 13-2

The decomposition process (breaking principles into conditions and consequences), the business-owner worked example, and the Apply vs. Strengthen distinction are taught in both 13-1 and 13-2 with nearly identical depth. The first lesson ends with a "Worked Example" and a "Matching Process" section that previews the 4-Step Method in 13-2.

**Recommendation:** Lesson 13-1 should focus on recognition and framing (question goal, why it matters, frequency, stem recognition, Apply vs. Strengthen contrast) and end with the Common Principle Structures table. Move the full decomposition process and worked example to 13-2, which can then open with those materials before the 4-Step Method.

### 3. Illustrative-Only Worked Examples in 13-1 and 13-2

Neither worked example presents actual answer choices. The student reads an explanation of what analysis would look like without ever evaluating any concrete options. Both lessons would benefit from a real question-card with answer choices — even a simplified one — so the student performs some independent work before encountering the solution.
