# Module 40: Title -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 2 website lessons (40-1 through 40-2) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 40 covers Title questions -- a low-frequency question type (17 appearances) that tests synthesis and scope judgment. The module is unique in addressing three distinct sub-types: Standard Title, Paired Titles, and Analogous Relationship titles. This adds genuine complexity that the lessons handle well:

1. **Zero practice questions** -- neither lesson includes a question-card or complete worked example.
2. **Three sub-types handled in two lessons** -- the Analogous Relationship sub-type is substantially more complex than Standard or Paired Titles, and all three are covered in only two lessons. The module handles this well but the Analogous sub-type deserves more space.
3. **Strong connection to Main Point** -- the module correctly notes the skill overlap with Main Point questions but does not provide an explicit comparison.
4. **The Analogous Relationship sub-type is uniquely challenging** -- it requires students to first define the relationship between passages and then find a pair of titles with the same abstract relationship. This is a two-layer abstraction task that gets only a brief treatment.
5. **Good scope-judgment framework** -- the Too Narrow/Too Broad/Wrong Focus elimination criteria in 40-2 are practical and well-suited to title evaluation.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 40-1: Germaine's Guide to RC Title Questions (~14 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction, concept overview, and stem identification guide.

**Strengths:**
- Clear opening that distinguishes the two main forms: Standard Title and Analogous Titles
- The numbered list (Standard vs. Analogous) with brief descriptions helps students quickly orient
- Three stem categories (Standard Title, Paired Titles, Analogous Relationship) are well-differentiated with real stem language
- The Overarching Takeaway correctly emphasizes scope and purpose matching
- Frequency note (17 appearances) sets appropriate expectations about rarity
- The cross-reference to Main Point questions helps students see the skill connection

**Issues:**
- **No practice question.** The three sub-types are introduced but never tested.
- **The Analogous Relationship sub-type is described in only one sentence** in the opening and one stem category. This is the hardest variant and needs more treatment.
- **No examples of good vs. bad titles.** The lesson describes what makes a good title (captures subject, scope, purpose) but does not show examples of titles that are too narrow, too broad, or wrong-focus.
- **No explicit comparison to Main Point or Passage Function.** Title, Main Point, and Passage Function form a natural cluster of "whole passage synthesis" question types.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add 2-3 example titles for a hypothetical passage: one correct (right scope), one too narrow, one too broad. This would concretize the scope-judgment framework.
- Expand the Analogous Relationship description with a worked walkthrough explaining the two-step process: define relationship, then find parallel pair.
- Add a brief "Title vs. Main Point vs. Passage Function" comparison note.

---

### Lesson 40-2: Step-by-Step Approach (~16 blocks)

**Role:** The complete method for solving Title questions, covering both standard and comparative variants.

**Strengths:**
- Correctly splits the method into two tracks: Standard Title (2 steps) and Paired/Analogous Title (2 steps)
- Standard Title Step 2's elimination criteria (Too Narrow, Too Broad, Wrong Focus) are practical and well-suited to the task
- The Paired/Analogous approach correctly emphasizes defining the relationship first, then matching the abstract structure
- The instruction "Apply the standard approach to both titles" for Paired Titles is efficient and avoids over-complication
- Tie-Breaker #1 (Verb Test) for standard titles teaches precision in evaluating title verbs
- Tie-Breaker #2 (Precision Check for Analogous) addresses the key difficulty: relationship definitions that are too vague

**Issues:**
- **No question-card.** Despite covering three sub-types, the lesson includes no practice.
- **No worked examples for any sub-type.** Neither Standard nor Analogous titles are demonstrated with actual title choices.
- **The Analogous track is complex but receives the same treatment length as the Standard track.** The two-layer abstraction (passage relationship → title pair relationship) deserves a dedicated worked example.
- **No common wrong-answer patterns for title questions.** Beyond Too Narrow/Too Broad/Wrong Focus, there are specific title traps (e.g., titles that use a word from the passage but misrepresent the scope) that are not addressed.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a worked example for Standard Titles: present a passage outline and 4-5 title options, demonstrating elimination.
- Add a separate worked example for Analogous Titles: define a passage relationship, then evaluate 4-5 title pairs.
- Add a question-card, ideally covering the Analogous variant since it is hardest and least practiced.
- Add a "title-specific trap" callout about titles that borrow passage vocabulary but misrepresent the scope.

---

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Practice Questions

Both lessons are pure instruction. Title questions are rare (17 appearances), so practice is especially important because students will not encounter them frequently in timed practice sets.

**Recommendation:** Add at least one question-card to 40-2, prioritizing the Analogous variant.

### 2. Analogous Titles Deserve More Space

The Analogous Relationship sub-type is the most complex variant in the entire RC question-type taxonomy. It requires two layers of abstraction and gets only brief treatment. For a question type that shows up on some tests, students who encounter it unprepared will lose significant time.

**Recommendation:** Either expand the Analogous treatment within 40-2 or create a dedicated Lesson 40-3 for Analogous Titles.
