# Module 29: Author Attitude -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 2 website lessons (29-1 through 29-2) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 29 covers Author Attitude questions in two lessons: Introduction (29-1) and Step-by-Step Approach (29-2). This is one of the stronger 2-lesson modules, with a clear teaching model centered on detecting "charged language" and characterizing tone by direction and intensity. Key improvement opportunities:

1. **No practice questions.** Neither lesson includes a question-card or worked example.
2. **Lesson 29-2's "Charged Language" hunt is the module's standout teaching tool** and deserves a concrete worked example.
3. **Overlap with Module 30 (Author Viewpoint) is not addressed.** The distinction between Attitude (tone/feeling) and Viewpoint (what the author would agree with) is critical but never explicitly drawn. Students will likely confuse these two question types.
4. **The tie-breaker strategies in 29-2 are excellent** -- the Synonym Test and the Extreme Word check address real sources of error.

---

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 29-1: Introduction (11 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction, stem identification, and importance statement.

**Strengths:**
- The four clue types for detecting attitude (word choice, degree of qualification, presentation of evidence, rhetorical structure) provide a concrete detection framework.
- Three stem categories (Direct Inquiry, Comparative Inquiry, Evidence of Attitude) cover the full range of attitude stems accurately.
- The "Evidence of Attitude" category is particularly valuable -- it asks students to identify which specific phrase reveals the author's tone, a skill many students lack.
- Frequency data (~100 times, ~5%) properly positions this as a moderately common question type.
- The importance section correctly notes that attitude mastery helps with Author Viewpoint and Main Conclusion questions.

**Issues:**
- **No distinction from Author Viewpoint (Module 30).** The importance section mentions that attitude helps with viewpoint questions but never explains how Attitude differs from Viewpoint. A brief callout would prevent confusion.
- **Redundancy.** The opening and the Overarching Takeaway both state the goal as identifying tone (positive/negative/neutral) and degree (strong/moderate).
- **No practice question.**

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a callout: "Attitude vs. Viewpoint: Attitude asks HOW the author feels (tone). Viewpoint asks WHAT the author believes (content)."
- Add a mini-exercise with sample sentences: "Is the author's tone positive, negative, or neutral?"

---

### Lesson 29-2: Step-by-Step Approach (12 blocks)

**Role:** The 4-step method plus two tie-breaker strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The 4-step method (Locate, Hunt for Charged Language, Determine Direction/Intensity, Match to Answers) is the clearest, most actionable method in any RC module reviewed so far.
- Step 2's "Hunt for Charged Language" with three specific targets (adjectives, adverbs, verbs) gives students a concrete detection checklist.
- Step 3's two-dimensional framework (Direction: positive/negative/neutral; Intensity: strong/moderate) is clean and easy to apply.
- Tie-Breaker #1 (The Synonym Test) addresses a real error source: students confusing related-but-different tone words (skepticism vs. disapproval).
- Tie-Breaker #2 (Find the Most Extreme Word) teaches students to calibrate answer intensity to passage language.

**Issues:**
- **No worked example.** The method is described clearly but never demonstrated on actual passage text. A brief excerpt with charged language identified and assessed would make the method concrete.
- **No question-card.**
- **Step 4 ("Match Your Assessment") is generic.** It could include a brief vocabulary of common tone words (e.g., "cautious optimism," "guarded skepticism," "mild disapproval") to help students translate their assessment into answer-choice language.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a worked example with 2-3 sentences of charged language, the direction/intensity assessment, and a matching answer choice.
- Add a question-card.
- Add a tone vocabulary list or table to Step 4.

---

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Practice Questions

The Charged Language detection skill is inherently hands-on. A worked example would be the single highest-impact addition to this module.

**Recommendation:** Add one question-card to Lesson 29-2.

### 2. No Attitude/Viewpoint Distinction

Modules 29 and 30 cover closely related question types. Without an explicit distinction, students may confuse them or apply the wrong method.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief callout to Lesson 29-1 explaining the difference (Attitude = tone; Viewpoint = belief content).
