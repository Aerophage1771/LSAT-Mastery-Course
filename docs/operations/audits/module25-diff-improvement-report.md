# Module 25: Inference -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 4 website lessons (25-1 through 25-4) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 25 covers Inference questions across four lessons: Introduction (25-1), Step-by-Step Approach (25-2), Pattern Recognition (25-3), and Trap Answer Types (25-4). This is a strong module with the best lesson balance of any RC question-type unit reviewed so far. The four-lesson arc -- introduce, method, patterns, traps -- is a clean progression. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **No practice questions in any lesson.** Despite Inference being nearly 30% of all RC questions, the entire module is theory-only. No question-cards, no worked examples, no exercises.
2. **Lesson 25-3 conflates pattern recognition with tactics.** It opens with three correct-answer patterns but then shifts to a "Tactics, Do's and Don'ts" section that belongs in a separate lesson or in the method lesson (25-2).
3. **Lesson 25-2 has a 5-step process that could be tighter.** Steps 1 and 2 ("Scan for stated Answer Choices" and "Look for Direct Matches") substantially overlap. Both tell the student to look for an answer that matches text they recall.
4. **The "Necessarily Consequenced" pattern name in 25-3 is awkward.** The term is not standard LSAT vocabulary and may confuse students.
5. **Lesson 25-4 includes a Speed Tip and Success Metrics section** that is useful but feels tacked on. These could live in a standalone reference or wrap-up lesson.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 25-1: Introduction (11 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction, stem identification, and importance statement.

**Strengths:**
- Strong opening mental model: "Because this text in the passage is true, I know this answer is true." This is the single best teaching sentence in the module.
- Four-category stem identification (Direct Question, Classic Inference, Evidence & Support, Negative Inference) covers the full range of inference stems with accurate examples.
- The Negative Inference category (EXCEPT/NOT) is valuable -- many students don't realize these are inference questions.
- Frequency data (~600 out of 2,098, nearly 30%) effectively communicates the importance of this question type.

**Issues:**
- **Mild redundancy between the opening and the Overarching Takeaway.** Both state the same "find what is provably true" principle. The opening paragraph and the callout could be consolidated.
- **No practice question.** For the most common RC question type, the introduction offers no opportunity to try an inference question.
- **The opening paragraph restates the question definition twice** -- once in the first sentence and again in the stem identification section's introductory paragraph.

**Suggested edits:**
- Cut the stem identification section's introductory paragraph (it repeats the opening).
- Add a lightweight exercise after the stem identification: "Which of these stems is an inference question?"

---

### Lesson 25-2: Step-by-Step Approach (13 blocks)

**Role:** The complete 5-step method for solving Inference questions.

**Strengths:**
- The 5-step process progresses logically from quick recall to deep analysis.
- Step 5 (Validate Indirect Inferences) is the lesson's best section -- the three inference types (Causality, Contrasts, Definitions & Examples) with signal words give students concrete tools.
- The signal word lists for each inference type are practical and actionable.

**Issues:**
- **Steps 1 and 2 overlap significantly.** Step 1 ("Scan for stated Answer Choices First") tells the student to find a match from memory. Step 2 ("Look for Direct Matches") tells the student to scan for close paraphrases. The difference is essentially "from memory" vs. "from re-reading" -- but this distinction isn't clearly articulated.
- **No worked example.** The method is described in five steps but never demonstrated on a real or sample passage.
- **No question-card.** The method lesson is entirely abstract.

**Suggested edits:**
- Merge Steps 1 and 2 into a single step ("Look for Direct Matches") and clarify when to use memory vs. re-reading.
- Add a worked example demonstrating the full process on a sample inference question.
- Add a question-card after the worked example.

---

### Lesson 25-3: Pattern Recognition (8 blocks)

**Role:** Teaches three correct-answer patterns and four tactical principles.

**Strengths:**
- The three correct-answer patterns (Two-Part Proof, Necessarily Consequenced, Cautious Wording) cover real inference answer structures.
- The "Cautious Wording Matches Cautious Evidence" pattern is especially valuable -- it teaches students to calibrate answer intensity to passage language.
- The Tactics breakdown (Evidence & Proof, Standard of Proof, Precision & Wording, Focus & Attribution) provides clear Do/Don't guidelines.

**Issues:**
- **Two distinct teaching goals in one lesson.** The lesson opens with three patterns (how correct answers look) then pivots to four tactical principles (Do's and Don'ts). These are different skills and could be separated.
- **"Necessarily Consequenced" is awkward phrasing.** The term isn't used in standard LSAT prep and may confuse students. Consider "Logical Necessity" or "Must-Be-True Deduction."
- **No examples for any pattern.** Each pattern is described abstractly. Even one concrete illustration per pattern would help.
- **No practice question.**

**Suggested edits:**
- Rename "Necessarily Consequenced" to "Logical Necessity" or "Must-Be-True Deduction."
- Add one concrete example for at least the "Two-Part Proof" pattern.
- Consider moving the Tactics section to Lesson 25-2 (as method-level guidance) or to a reference lesson.

---

### Lesson 25-4: Trap Answer Types (10 blocks)

**Role:** Teaches five trap types plus speed tips and success metrics.

**Strengths:**
- Five trap types (Too Extreme, Out of Scope, Reversed Relationship, Misattributed View, One Word Off) are well-chosen and cover the real distractor landscape for inference questions.
- The "One Word Off" trap is particularly valuable -- it addresses a subtle error type that many students miss.
- The Speed Tip callout (eliminate traps first) is practical and actionable.
- Success Metrics provide a concrete self-assessment framework (90% accuracy, confidence in elimination, process-driven thinking).

**Issues:**
- **No examples for any trap type.** Each trap is defined with a signal description but never illustrated.
- **The Speed Tip and Success Metrics sections feel like separate content.** They address test strategy and self-assessment, not trap identification. They could belong in a reference or wrap-up lesson.
- **No practice question.** After learning five traps, the student has no chance to identify which trap a given wrong answer represents.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add one concrete example for each trap type.
- Consider whether the Speed Tip and Success Metrics belong here or in a separate reference lesson.
- Add a mini-exercise: "Identify the trap in each of these wrong answers."

---

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Practice Questions in the Entire Module

The same critical gap as Module 24. Inference questions represent ~30% of all RC questions -- the largest single category -- and the entire module is theory-only.

**Recommendation:** Add at least two question-cards: one in Lesson 25-2 (method demonstration) and one in Lesson 25-4 (trap identification).

### 2. Lesson 25-3 Mixes Two Teaching Goals

The pattern recognition lesson tries to cover both correct-answer patterns and tactical Do's/Don'ts. These serve different functions and would be clearer as separate sections or lessons.

**Recommendation:** Move the Tactics section to Lesson 25-2 (the method lesson) or to a standalone reference.

### 3. Steps 1-2 in Lesson 25-2 Overlap

The first two steps of the 5-step method are nearly identical in their instruction. This creates a 5-step process that is functionally 4 steps.

**Recommendation:** Merge Steps 1 and 2 into a single step with a sub-note about memory-first vs. scan-first approaches.

## Summary of Priorities

1. **Add practice questions to the module** -- critical gap for the most common RC question type
2. **Add a worked example to Lesson 25-2** -- the method lesson needs demonstration
3. **Merge Steps 1-2 in Lesson 25-2** -- reduce redundancy in the method
4. **Rename "Necessarily Consequenced" in Lesson 25-3** -- use standard terminology
5. **Add "try it first" prompts before any future worked analyses** -- prime active learning
