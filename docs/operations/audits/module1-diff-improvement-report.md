# Module 1: Argument Part -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 12 website lessons (1-1 through 1-12) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 1 is a strong foundational unit with clear teaching progressions, accurate LSAT content, and well-chosen examples. The 12 lessons cover introduction through advanced concepts, and lessons 1-4 through 1-11 include real PT question cards with full worked analyses. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **Lesson 1-1 tries to do too much** -- it covers introduction, anatomy, all six roles, AND the indicator reference. This is ~49 blocks serving as both overview and standalone reference. Split or tighten.
2. **Lesson 1-3 heavily overlaps 1-1** -- both teach the six roles with nearly identical content. One should reference the other, not repeat it.
3. **No practice questions in the first 3 lessons** -- the student doesn't encounter a question-card until Lesson 1-4. The opening lessons are all lecture with no hands-on practice.
4. **Advanced lessons (1-7 through 1-11) are excellent** -- each has a focused concept, a real PT question, and a full wrong-answer analysis. This is the gold standard the earlier lessons should match.
5. **The reference guide (1-12) is too thin** -- only 21 blocks for a complete reference. It should be the definitive one-stop cheat sheet but currently lacks trap answer patterns and worked examples.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 1-1: MODULE 1: Argument Part (49 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction and overview.

**Strengths:**
- Clear Question Goal statement up front
- The "Why This Matters" section effectively motivates the topic
- The movie example (rain/movies) is simple, relatable, and correctly analyzed
- The Why Test callout is the single best teaching moment in the lesson
- Indicator Words Reference table is useful and accurate
- Strong Key Takeaway callout closes the lesson

**Issues:**
- **Scope creep:** The lesson covers introduction, anatomy, all six roles, flow of support, the Why Test, AND the indicator word reference. That's five distinct teaching goals in one lesson.
- **Six Roles section (blocks 24-43) duplicates Lesson 1-3** almost verbatim. Both teach the same six roles with the same "Spotting It" callouts. This means a student encounters the same content twice without new depth the second time.
- **No practice question.** The student reads 49 blocks of theory without ever being asked to try anything. Even a single lightweight question after the movie example would improve retention.
- **"What Makes This Question Type Unique" (blocks 6-8)** is useful but could be more direct. The two paragraphs say the same thing: "you describe, not evaluate." One paragraph is enough.

**Suggested edits:**
- Cut the Six Roles section (blocks 24-43) entirely. Let Lesson 1-3 own that content. Replace with a forward reference: "The six roles are covered in depth in Lesson 3."
- Cut one of the two "unique" paragraphs (block 7 or 8).
- Add a mini-exercise after the movie example breakdown: "Try it yourself: identify the role of each sentence in this argument: [short stimulus]."
- The Indicator Words Reference table (block 47) could move to the reference guide (1-12) or stay here as a preview -- either way, note it repeats later.

### Lesson 1-2: The 5-Step Method (74 blocks)

**Role:** The complete method for solving Argument Part questions.

**Strengths:**
- Excellent structure: overview process block, then each step gets its own H2 section with example, tip callout, and "when it gets tricky" callout.
- The timing callout (1:25 per question) is valuable and unique to this lesson.
- Prephrase examples table (block 46) is one of the strongest teaching tools in the module -- shows weak vs. strong prephrases side by side.
- Full Worked Example at the end (film critic/theater attendance) walks through all 5 steps coherently.
- "When the Method Gets Hard" breakdown (block 72) addresses 5 real complications students face.

**Issues:**
- **Length.** At 74 blocks, this is the longest lesson in the module. Steps 1-5 are each thorough, but the total reading time may cause students to lose focus before reaching the worked example.
- **Step 2 example (Vitamin D)** is good but disconnected from the worked example (film critic). Using the same stimulus across Steps 1-5 would create a running thread the student follows from start to finish.
- **No question-card.** Despite being the method lesson, there's no interactive practice. The worked example is a blockquote + analysis, not a question-card the student can attempt before seeing the answer.
- **The Two-Part Check (block 54)** for Step 5 is important but buried late. Consider promoting this concept earlier or making it a standalone callout.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a question-card after the Full Worked Example section, giving the student one chance to apply the method independently.
- Consider using the same argument across all 5 steps (instead of switching between carbon emissions, Vitamin D, traffic/rain, and film critic). A unified running example would be easier to follow.
- Tighten Step 2 and Step 3 -- they have overlapping advice about indicator words.
- The "When This Step Gets Tricky" callouts are great individually but add up to a lot of edge-case detail. Consider moving some to the Reference Guide.

### Lesson 1-3: Common Roles & Patterns (80 blocks)

**Role:** Detailed role catalog with frequency data and pattern recognition.

**Strengths:**
- The frequency table (block 6) with exact percentages (53.5%, 13.2%, 11.1%, 9.0%, 8.3%, 4.9%) is excellent -- gives students a clear prioritization framework.
- The 80/20 Rule callout ("78% of questions target just three roles") is the single most actionable insight in the module.
- Each role gets a concrete blockquote example with analysis.
- The Pattern Recognition section (blocks 60-76) teaching Simple, Chain, and Rebuttal patterns is pedagogically strong.
- Quick Recognition Guide table (block 58) is a useful cheat sheet.

**Issues:**
- **Heavy overlap with Lesson 1-1.** Blocks 8-55 (the six roles) duplicate 1-1's blocks 24-43 with only slightly more depth. A student who reads both lessons encounters the same definitions, same indicator words, and same "spotting it" tips twice.
- **80 blocks is very long** for a single lesson. The combination of role catalog + pattern recognition + quick reference is three distinct lessons packed into one.
- **No question-card.** This lesson introduces all six roles and three argument patterns without a single practice opportunity. At minimum, the pattern recognition section should include a short exercise for each pattern.

**Suggested edits:**
- If Lesson 1-1 is trimmed (see above), this lesson becomes the definitive role reference -- no overlap.
- Add one question-card or mini-exercise after the Pattern Recognition section.
- Consider splitting: Roles (1-3a) and Patterns (1-3b) as separate lessons if length is a concern.
- The Context/Framing breakdown (block 37, 5 items) is richer here than in 1-1. Good -- this should be the canonical version.

### Lesson 1-4: Concept Focus -- Core Components (23 blocks)

**Role:** Focused concept lesson on premises vs. conclusions.

**Strengths:**
- Tight and focused -- 23 blocks, one clear goal.
- Includes a real question-card (PT-107-S-4-Q-15, easy difficulty).
- Step-by-step analysis breakdown after the question is thorough.
- The "Abstract Pattern" section at the end (block 21) generalizes the lesson to future questions.

**Issues:**
- **This is the first question-card in the module.** Students have read 3 full lessons (~200 blocks) of pure theory before encountering their first practice question. This is too late.
- The content (premises vs. conclusions, direction of support) overlaps with 1-1 and 1-3. By Lesson 4, the student has been told "premises support conclusions" three times.
- The wrong-answer analysis uses a 5-item breakdown with Correct/Incorrect badges (block 18), which works but differs from the "Why Each Wrong Answer Fails" heading + breakdown format used in 1-5 through 1-11. The content is solid — each wrong answer gets a clear explanation of why it fails.

**Suggested edits:**
- Consider whether the theoretical content (blocks 0-13) is needed at all, since 1-1 and 1-3 already cover premises vs. conclusions. This lesson could open with a shorter setup and get to the question-card faster.
- Minor formatting: aligning the wrong-answer breakdown to the same heading style as 1-5 through 1-11 would improve consistency across the module.

### Lesson 1-5: The Intermediate Conclusion (37 blocks)

**Role:** Focused concept lesson on intermediate conclusions.

**Strengths:**
- Clear conceptual hook: "the dual nature test" is memorable and actionable.
- Good comparison table (block 12): Receives Support? / Gives Support? / Position in Chain.
- Real question-card (PT-112-S-4-Q-13) with full analysis.
- Includes "Why Each Wrong Answer Fails" section (block 31) -- the gold standard format.
- Two verification techniques at the end (Removal Test, Double-Arrow Check) give students concrete tools.

**Issues:**
- **Lesson title could be more specific.** "The Intermediate Conclusion" is accurate but doesn't signal that this is a *practice* lesson with a worked example. Something like "The Intermediate Conclusion: Concept + Practice" would set expectations.
- The opening duplicates the dual nature test from 1-1 (callout block 34) -- but this is acceptable since 1-5 goes deeper.

**Suggested edits:**
- Minor: the lesson has both `h2` and `h3` headers for the same concept ("The Intermediate Conclusion" appears as both). Consolidate to one.
- This lesson is well-structured. It's a model for what lessons 1-4, 1-1, and 1-3 should aspire to.

### Lesson 1-6: The Opposing Viewpoint (33 blocks)

**Role:** Focused concept lesson on opposing viewpoints and rebuttal structure.

**Strengths:**
- The Rebuttal Pattern process block (4 steps) is clear and correct.
- Attribution Phrases table and Pivot Words table with strength gradations are excellent reference material.
- Real question-card (PT-129-S-2-Q-13) with full worked analysis.
- "Why Each Wrong Answer Fails" breakdown present.
- The "Common Trap" callout about confusing opposing view with author's conclusion addresses a real, frequent student error.

**Issues:**
- **Overlap with 1-1 and 1-3.** The opposing viewpoint was already defined in both earlier lessons. This lesson adds the rebuttal pattern and attribution/pivot tables, which is genuine new depth -- but the opening paragraphs repeat prior definitions.
- The "Distinguishing Author from Opponent" list (block 19, 4 items) is a critical skill that deserves more emphasis. Currently it's a bullet list that could be a table or breakdown with examples.

**Suggested edits:**
- Open with the rebuttal pattern directly instead of re-defining the opposing viewpoint. Reference 1-1/1-3 for the definition.
- Expand the "Distinguishing Author from Opponent" list into a worked mini-example.

### Lesson 1-7: Advanced Concepts -- Difficult Questions (37 blocks)

**Role:** Bridge from basic to advanced, covering complexity sources.

**Strengths:**
- Clearly identifies three difficulty sources: logic complexity, abstract language, strategic moves.
- The Strategic Moves table (block 15) naming counterexample, concession, reductio, and thought experiment is valuable -- these are real LSAT patterns.
- The Substitution Technique (callout block 23) for decoding abstract answer language is practical and actionable.
- Abstract Language translation table (block 22) is one of the most useful tools in the module.
- Hard Question Protocol (process block 34, 5 steps) gives a concrete fallback strategy.

**Issues:**
- **No question-card.** This is the one advanced lesson without a practice question. The "Worked Example: Abstract Answer Choices" section (blocks 25-30) refers to an art critic argument but doesn't include the actual question-card -- just the analysis. The student can't attempt the question before seeing the answer.
- The lesson tries to cover three distinct advanced topics. Each could be its own mini-lesson.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add the missing question-card for the art critic worked example so students can attempt it before the analysis.
- Consider whether this lesson should be split into: (a) Complex Logic, (b) Abstract Language. The strategic moves content could be folded into the relevant individual lessons (e.g., concession strategy → Lesson 1-8).

**Role:** Four focused advanced lessons, each covering one specific role/concept.

- This consistency makes the lessons predictable and easy to navigate.

### Lesson 1-12: Complete Reference (21 blocks)

**Role:** One-stop cheat sheet for the entire module.

**Strengths:**
- Covers the 5-Step Method, all roles, indicator words, common structures, and common traps.
- Compact -- a student can skim this in 2-3 minutes before a practice section.

**Issues:**
- **Too thin.** At 21 blocks, this is the shortest lesson in the module. For a "Complete Reference," it's missing several things covered in earlier lessons:
  - No abstract language translation table (from 1-7)
  - No attribution phrases / pivot words table (from 1-6)
  - No frequency data (from 1-3)
  - No "Why Each Wrong Answer Fails" pattern summary
  - No prephrase examples (from 1-2)
- **The "Common Traps" breakdown (block 19)** has only 4 items. The advanced lessons identify many more specific traps.
- **No question-card.** A reference guide doesn't need one, but a single quick-check exercise would help students verify they can apply the reference.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add the abstract language translation table from 1-7.
- Add the frequency table from 1-3 (or at least the 80/20 rule callout).
- Add prephrase examples from 1-2.
- Expand the Common Traps section to include traps from the advanced lessons (hypothetical trap from 1-10, abstract language trap from 1-9, etc.).
- Consider adding a "Diagnostic Checklist" process block: a 5-item pre-answer checklist students can use on test day.

## Structural Issues

### 1. Redundancy Between Lessons 1-1 and 1-3

The six roles are taught in full twice: once in 1-1 (blocks 24-43) and again in 1-3 (blocks 8-55). Both include the same definitions, the same "Spotting It" callouts, and nearly identical indicator words. This is the module's biggest structural problem.

**Recommendation:** Lesson 1-1 should reference the roles briefly (one table, no sub-sections) and point to 1-3 for the full treatment. Or: 1-1 covers only the top 3 roles as a preview, and 1-3 covers all six.

### 2. Practice Drought in Lessons 1-1 through 1-3

The first three lessons contain ~200 blocks of theory and zero practice questions. The first question-card appears in Lesson 1-4. Students who learn best by doing will disengage before they get a chance to try anything.

**Recommendation:** Add at least one lightweight exercise (not necessarily a full question-card) to each of the first three lessons. Even "identify the conclusion in this 3-sentence argument" in a callout would help.

### 3. Lesson 1-7 Is an Orphan

Lesson 1-7 (Advanced Concepts) covers three topics that are then taught individually in 1-8 through 1-11. It functions as an overview/preview of the advanced section. But it's also the only advanced lesson without a question-card, making it the weakest link in an otherwise strong advanced sequence.

**Recommendation:** Either add a question-card to 1-7, or fold its content into the individual advanced lessons and remove it as a standalone lesson. The abstract language content could go to the Reference Guide.

## Summary of Priorities

1. **Eliminate 1-1 / 1-3 role duplication** -- the biggest structural fix
2. **Add practice to lessons 1-1, 1-2, 1-3** -- closes the theory-only gap
3. **Enrich the Reference Guide (1-12)** -- it should be the definitive cheat sheet
4. **Add a question-card to Lesson 1-7** -- the only advanced lesson without one
5. **Add "try it first" prompts before every worked analysis** -- small change, big pedagogical impact
