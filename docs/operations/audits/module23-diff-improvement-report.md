# Module 59: LSAT Math -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 7 website lessons (59-1 through 59-7) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 59 is a well-organized conceptual unit that teaches quantitative reasoning flaws across six flaw families. The teaching is accurate, the examples are relevant, and the flaw taxonomy is comprehensive. However, the module has significant structural weaknesses:

1. **Zero real LSAT question-cards in the entire module.** Not one of the seven lessons includes an actual PT question for the student to attempt. Every "practice" element is either an illustrative example with the answer immediately visible, or an accordion containing only the analysis of an unseen question. This is the module's most critical gap.
2. **No "try it first" prompts anywhere.** The few practice-adjacent sections (59-4 Practice Question, 59-5 Practice, 59-6 Advanced Practice) jump directly to analysis without inviting the student to attempt anything first.
3. **Lesson 59-3 (Averages) is extremely thin** -- only ~10 content blocks covering three distinct sub-topics. It is the shortest substantive lesson in the module by a wide margin.
4. **Lesson 59-4 has a broken practice question** -- the "Practice Question" heading exists but the actual stimulus and answer choices are missing; only the analysis callout remains.
5. **Heavy overlap between Lesson 59-6 (Conclusion) and Lesson 59-7 (Reference Guide)** -- both contain a complete flaw-type catalog, signal words, common traps, and a test-day checklist. The content is near-identical in places.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 59-1: The Foundational Toolkit (~25 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction and vocabulary builder.

**Strengths:**
- Clear two-part module framing: build the foundation, identify the traps.
- The Cross-Question Reach callout showing how quantitative flaws surface in Flaw, Weaken, Strengthen, and Paradox questions is excellent -- it immediately establishes the cross-cutting importance of the topic.
- The 10-concept breakdown is thorough and each entry includes both a definition and an LSAT Trap, which is exactly the right pairing for applied learning.
- The Raw Numbers vs. Percentages vs. Rates comparison table is one of the best teaching tools in the module -- the "What It Hides" and "Classic LSAT Swap" columns are directly actionable.
- The Golden Rule callout is a strong heuristic: premise in one unit, conclusion in another = red flag.
- The Master Table of LSAT Math Flaw Types provides a clear roadmap for the rest of the module.

**Issues:**
- **No practice of any kind.** The student reads 25 blocks of definitions and tables without ever applying them. Even a brief "which concept is being misused?" exercise after the 10-concept breakdown would improve retention.
- **The module header says "MODULE 22"** in the website version but "MODULE 23" in the mastery course main content. This numbering inconsistency should be resolved.
- **The flaw roadmap table lists only six flaw families but excludes** several that appear later (Double Counting, Percentage of a Percentage, Net Effect). This creates a gap between the roadmap and the actual content of Lessons 4 and 6.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a mini-exercise after the Raw Numbers vs. Percentages vs. Rates table: present a short argument and ask the student to identify which type of measure the premise uses and which the conclusion uses.
- Reconcile the module numbering (22 vs. 23).
- Either expand the roadmap table to include all flaw sub-types, or note explicitly that some lessons cover multiple flaws.

### Lesson 59-2: The Part vs. The Whole (~40 blocks)

**Role:** Covers two flaw families -- Numbers vs. Percentages and Part vs. Whole.

**Strengths:**
- Clear structure: the lesson separates the two fallacy types (Absolute Number vs. Rate, then Part vs. Whole) with distinct sections for each.
- The Ski Injury Case callout is an outstanding worked example -- it uses concrete numbers (18% of 10,000 vs. 26% of 5,000) to make the flaw viscerally clear.
- The Spotting the Swap diagnostic table (Premise States / Conclusion Claims / Flaw?) is a practical, reusable tool.
- The Composition vs. Division breakdown correctly identifies both directions of the Part vs. Whole fallacy with clear examples.
- Three LSAT-style application examples (Restaurant, Electric Car, University Budget) cover Part-to-Whole and Whole-to-Part in realistic contexts.
- The "How This Flaw Appears Across Question Types" breakdown mapping the fallacy to Flaw, Weaken, Strengthen, and Paradox questions is excellent for building transferable skill.

**Issues:**
- **No question-card.** Despite having a "Practice: Spot the Flaw" section at the end, there is no actual question for the student to attempt. The section simply describes a scenario (school honors program) and tells the student what the flaw is. This defeats the purpose of practice.
- **Covering two flaw families in one lesson** makes it the longest substantive lesson (~40 blocks). The Numbers vs. Percentages content and the Part vs. Whole content could each sustain a focused lesson.
- The three LSAT-style applications are all analyzed immediately -- no opportunity for the student to attempt analysis before reading the answer.

**Suggested edits:**
- Convert the "Practice: Spot the Flaw" section into an actual exercise: present the honors-program argument as a blockquote and ask the student to identify the flaw before revealing the analysis.
- Add "Try this" prompts before each of the three Application analyses.
- Consider splitting into two lessons if length is a concern in the alternate.

### Lesson 59-3: The Averages Fallacy (~10 blocks)

**Role:** Covers Simpson's Paradox, weighted averages, and mean vs. median.

**Strengths:**
- The Simpson's Paradox table with actual numbers (Drug vs. Placebo, Mild vs. Severe) is clear and lets the student see the reversal concretely.
- Three distinct sub-topics (Simpson's Paradox, Weighted Averages, Mean vs. Median) are all relevant LSAT flaw types.
- The Practice Snapshot at the end (school board / district-wide average) is a good illustrative example.

**Issues:**
- **Far too short.** At roughly 10 content blocks, this lesson is dramatically thinner than every other lesson in the module. Lesson 59-2 is approximately 4x longer for a comparable number of flaw sub-types.
- **Three major topics get one paragraph each.** Weighted Averages and Mean vs. Median each receive a single paragraph of explanation with no examples. Simpson's Paradox gets the table but no LSAT-contextualized application showing how it appears in a stimulus.
- **No question-card.** The Practice Snapshot presents an example but immediately reveals the full analysis in a callout -- no opportunity for the student to reason through it.
- **Missing: how these flaws appear across question types.** Lesson 59-2 maps its flaws to Flaw/Weaken/Strengthen/Paradox question types; this lesson does not. The omission is inconsistent.
- **Missing: LSAT signal words for averages.** No equivalent of the Spotting the Swap table from Lesson 59-2.

**Suggested edits:**
- Expand each sub-topic with at least one LSAT-contextualized example: a realistic stimulus showing the flaw in action.
- Add a diagnostic table or signal-word list for average-related arguments.
- Add a "How This Flaw Appears Across Question Types" section paralleling Lesson 59-2.
- Add a question-card or at minimum a "try it first" prompt before the Practice Snapshot analysis.
- This lesson needs roughly 20-25 more blocks to match the depth standard set by Lessons 59-2 and 59-5.

### Lesson 59-4: Invalid Mathematical Reasoning (~12 blocks)

**Role:** Covers four math errors: absolute vs. relative change, double-counting, base-rate neglect, and percentage-of-a-percentage.

**Strengths:**
- The four error types are well-chosen and each is a genuine LSAT pattern.
- Each sub-topic gets a punchy, example-driven explanation that makes the flaw immediately understandable.
- The percentage-of-a-percentage example (100 -> 50 -> 75) is simple and effective.
- The Practice Question analysis callout correctly identifies base-rate neglect and walks through the wrong answers.

**Issues:**
- **The Practice Question stimulus is missing.** There is an h4 header "Practice Question" followed directly by a "Full Analysis" callout that references answer choices (A) through (E) and a journalist's argument about factory workers. But the actual stimulus and answer choices are nowhere in the lesson. The student sees only the solution. This is a critical content gap.
- **Short overall.** At ~12 blocks, four major error types each get only a single paragraph. Base-rate neglect in particular is a concept that deserves a worked numerical example (like the Simpson's Paradox table in 59-3).
- **No "How This Appears Across Question Types" mapping.** Present in 59-2 but missing here.
- **The title says "Lesson 4" but actually covers four distinct flaw sub-types** that the Master Table in Lesson 1 assigns across multiple categories. The lesson tries to be a grab-bag, which dilutes focus.

**Suggested edits:**
- Restore or add the missing practice question stimulus and answer choices. This is the highest-priority fix for this lesson.
- Add a base-rate neglect worked example with actual numbers (e.g., 1/10,000 prevalence, 99% accurate test, compute the actual probability of infection given a positive result).
- Add a "try it first" prompt before the Practice Question analysis.
- Consider adding brief LSAT application examples for double-counting and percentage-of-a-percentage.

### Lesson 59-5: Flawed Predictions / The Gambler's Fallacy (~35 blocks)

**Role:** Covers the Gambler's Fallacy, the Hot Hand Fallacy, and the distinction between independent and dependent events.

**Strengths:**
- This is the strongest lesson in the module. It is thorough, well-structured, and builds logically from concept to LSAT application.
- The Gambler's Fallacy definition, everyday example, and LSAT example (Meteorite Case) form a clear teaching progression.
- The Emotional Hook callout identifying signal words ("overdue," "bound to happen," "due for a correction") is directly actionable on test day.
- The Hot Hand Fallacy is correctly presented as the mirror image, and the comparison callout (Gambler's vs. Hot Hand) makes the distinction memorable.
- The Independent vs. Dependent Events breakdown is the pedagogical core of the lesson and is well-executed, with examples of each category and the critical LSAT skill stated explicitly.
- The three-category list (Independent / Dependent / Ambiguous) with the volcano example is excellent -- it shows how the LSAT exploits real-world complexity.
- The 5-pattern process block (Due, Streak, Average as Schedule, Reversed Conditional, Frequency as Certainty) is a comprehensive recognition guide.
- The Practice section includes an accordion with full wrong-answer analysis.

**Issues:**
- **No actual question-card.** The accordion "Full Analysis of Practice Question" discusses answer choices (A) through (E) for an earthquake/official scenario, but the stimulus and answer choices are not present in the lesson. The student cannot attempt the question.
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The accordion is the only barrier between the student and the answer, but there is no explicit invitation to attempt the question first.
- The lesson is comprehensive but long. At ~35 blocks it is the longest lesson in the module. This is acceptable given the quality, but the lack of any interactive practice element is more glaring in a long lesson.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add the missing practice question stimulus and answer choices before the accordion analysis.
- Add a "Try this: Attempt the question before reading the analysis below." prompt.
- If the stimulus cannot be included (e.g., copyright), convert the Practice section into a constructed example with visible answer choices.

### Lesson 59-6: Module Conclusion (~50 blocks)

**Role:** Consolidation lesson covering multi-flaw arguments, signal words, and a test-day routine.

**Strengths:**
- The four Common Combinations (Percentage + Part vs. Whole, Average + Gambler's, Overlapping Sets + Net Effect, Rate vs. Absolute + Inverse Probability) are well-constructed and realistic.
- The Multi-Flaw Strategy callout ("When you spot one flaw, don't stop") is a valuable advanced tip.
- The 10-row Signal Words and Patterns table is comprehensive and includes the "Flaw to Check" column, making it immediately actionable.
- The Advanced Practice accordion includes a multi-flaw argument with a primary/secondary flaw analysis -- this is exactly the level of sophistication an advanced student needs.
- The Complete LSAT Math Flaw Reference breakdown (10 items) summarizes all flaw types with cues and diagnostic questions.
- The 4-step Quantitative Reasoning Checklist is a clean test-day routine.

**Issues:**
- **Missing practice stimulus.** Like Lessons 59-4 and 59-5, the Advanced Practice accordion references answer choices (A) through (E) but the actual question stimulus and choices are absent. The student can only read the analysis.
- **Heavy overlap with Lesson 59-7 (Reference Guide).** The Complete LSAT Math Flaw Reference in this lesson covers the same 10 flaw types as the Master Flaw Types Table in 59-7. The Signal Words table here overlaps substantially with the Signal Words Table in 59-7. The Quantitative Reasoning Checklist here (4 steps) overlaps with the Test Day Checklist in 59-7 (7 steps). A student who reads both lessons encounters the same content twice.
- **No "try it first" prompt** before the Advanced Practice analysis.
- The lesson is ~50 blocks long, which is appropriate for a conclusion/consolidation, but the redundancy with 59-7 means much of that length is duplicative rather than additive.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add the missing Advanced Practice stimulus and answer choices.
- Reduce overlap with 59-7 by one of: (a) removing the Flaw Reference and Signal Words from this lesson and pointing to 59-7, or (b) keeping the conclusion focused on multi-flaw strategy and the checklist while the reference guide owns the catalogs.
- Add a "try it first" prompt before the Advanced Practice analysis.

### Lesson 59-7: Reference Guide (~40 blocks)

**Role:** Comprehensive one-stop reference for all LSAT Math concepts.

**Strengths:**
- The Master Flaw Types Table (8 rows) is the most complete flaw catalog in the module, adding Double Counting, Relative vs. Absolute Change, and Percentage of a Percentage that were missing from the Lesson 1 roadmap.
- The Signal Words Table organized by category (Proportional, Aggregate, Central Tendency, etc.) is clean and scannable.
- The Question Types interaction table showing how math flaws appear in Flaw, Weaken, Strengthen, Paradox, and Must Be True questions is valuable and well-structured.
- The 7 Common Traps breakdown with "How it works / How to beat it" is excellent -- each trap is named memorably and the countermeasure is actionable.
- The 7-step Test Day Checklist is the most thorough version in the module.
- The B-A-R-G-D-P-R-P mnemonic is a useful recall tool.

**Issues:**
- **Heavy overlap with Lesson 59-6.** As noted above, the flaw catalog, signal words, and checklist appear in both lessons. The reference guide should be the canonical source; the conclusion lesson should reference it rather than duplicating it.
- **No worked example or quick-check exercise.** A reference guide does not require a full question-card, but a single "Quick Check" exercise (e.g., "Name the flaw in this 2-sentence argument") would help students verify they can apply the reference.
- **Missing from the reference:** the 10-concept vocabulary from Lesson 1 (Absolute Number, Rate, Total, Percentage, Average, Median, Weighted Average, Rate of Change, Market Share, Threshold). The reference guide catalogues flaws but not the foundational terms those flaws exploit.
- **Missing: base-rate neglect numerical example.** The flaw description in the table is conceptual; a worked-out probability calculation would make it concrete.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a condensed version of the 10-concept vocabulary table from Lesson 1.
- Add one quick-check exercise at the end.
- Deduplicate with Lesson 59-6 by making this the single canonical flaw/signal/checklist reference.

## Structural Issues

### 1. Zero Question-Cards Across the Entire Module

This is the module's most critical weakness. None of the seven lessons includes an actual LSAT question with stimulus and answer choices for the student to attempt. Lessons 59-4, 59-5, and 59-6 contain analysis of practice questions, but the questions themselves are absent -- the student can only read the solutions.

**Recommendation:** Add at least one real or constructed question-card to Lessons 59-2, 59-3, 59-4, and 59-5. Restore the missing stimuli for the existing practice analyses. Lesson 59-1 should have a lightweight exercise. Lesson 59-6 should have its Advanced Practice stimulus restored.

### 2. No "Try It First" Prompts

None of the seven lessons includes any prompt inviting the student to attempt an example before reading the analysis. Even the existing worked examples (Ski Injury Case in 59-2, Practice Snapshot in 59-3, Application examples in 59-2) reveal the answer immediately.

**Recommendation:** Add "Try this: Attempt the question before reading the analysis below." before every worked analysis section across all lessons.

### 3. Lesson 59-3 Is Drastically Underweight

At ~10 blocks, Lesson 59-3 (Averages) is roughly 4x shorter than the next shortest substantive lesson. It covers three important sub-topics (Simpson's Paradox, Weighted Averages, Mean vs. Median) with insufficient depth. This creates an uneven learning experience -- the student spends 40 blocks on Part vs. Whole but only 10 on Averages.

**Recommendation:** Expand Lesson 59-3 to ~25-30 blocks with additional examples, LSAT applications, signal words, and a diagnostic table.

### 4. Heavy Redundancy Between Lessons 59-6 and 59-7

The Conclusion and Reference Guide overlap substantially in flaw catalogs, signal words, and checklists. A student who reads both encounters much of the same content twice without new depth.

**Recommendation:** The Reference Guide (59-7) should be the single canonical source for catalogs and checklists. The Conclusion (59-6) should focus on multi-flaw combinations, advanced strategy, and forward references to the reference guide.

### 5. Missing Practice Stimuli in Lessons 59-4, 59-5, 59-6

Three lessons contain "Full Analysis" sections that reference specific answer choices and stimuli that are not present in the lesson content. These appear to be remnants of content that was removed or never included.

**Recommendation:** Either restore the original stimuli and answer choices, or replace them with constructed examples that include visible stimuli and answers.
