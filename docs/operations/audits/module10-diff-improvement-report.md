# Module 10: Sufficient Assumption -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 12 website lessons (10-1 through 10-12) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 10 is an exceptionally well-structured unit. The content is logically accurate, the teaching progression is deliberate, and the drill lessons (10-3 through 10-11) each follow a near-identical high-quality pattern: concept explanation, practice question, full step-by-step analysis, and a visual summary. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **No "try it first" prompts in any drill lesson.** Every question-card is followed immediately by the analysis, without any pause for students to attempt the question independently. This is the module's most consistent and most impactful gap.
2. **Lesson 10-1 introduction example is described, not shown.** The worked example in the introductory lesson is a clean conditional argument, but the lesson explains it without presenting it as a question-card. Students leave the introduction without ever attempting a real question.
3. **Lesson 10-2 worked example has no question-card.** The "Full Worked Example" section (the restaurant critic argument) provides a step-by-step walkthrough but no opportunity for the student to attempt the question before seeing the analysis.
4. **Lessons 10-3 through 10-11 are genuinely excellent** -- each has a focused pattern, a real PT question, and a full wrong-answer analysis. Minor improvements are available but these lessons are the gold standard the opening lessons should aspire to.
5. **Reference Guide (10-12) is comprehensive but misses two items** from the module: the "Causal Sufficient Assumption" and "Existing Rule / Missing Trigger" patterns, which appear in the mastery course but not in the website reference. The website's Test Day Checklist is strong.

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 10-1: MODULE 10: Sufficient Assumption (approx. 30 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction -- question goal, frequency, stem recognition, SA vs. NA, the logical gap, and recurring patterns.

**Strengths:**
- The Question Goal statement is precise and immediately distinguishes SA from Strengthen.
- The SA vs. NA comparison table is the best teaching tool in the lesson. The "steel beam vs. support cable" analogy is memorable and conceptually accurate.
- The "Logical Gap" section is particularly strong: it names four structural gap types (new concept, broken chain, scope expansion, unstated classification) with clear descriptions.
- The Common SA Patterns table at the end gives students a taxonomy they can apply immediately to the drill lessons that follow.
- The frequency callout (1--2 per section, second half of section) is practically useful.

**Issues:**
- **The worked example is illustrative, not interactive.** The Sandra/leadership training example is accurate and well-chosen, but the lesson simply walks through it in text blocks (Steps 1--4 as paragraphs). There is no question-card presenting the argument with five answer choices. A student cannot practice the method before the answer is revealed.
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The analysis of the Sandra example appears without any invitation for the student to attempt the question. This is a missed retention opportunity for the only piece of practice in the lesson.
- **The SA vs. NA callout ("The Relationship Between SA and NA")** makes a precise logical point: every SA is also a NA, but not vice versa. This is correct and valuable. However, the claim appears in text but is easy to miss. It deserves a standalone callout or table row.
- **Module heading says "MODULE 10" in the lesson but the mastery course titles it Module 11** in the introduction h2 -- the website lesson correctly uses "MODULE 10." Minor mismatch between versions.

**Suggested edits:**
- Convert the Sandra example into a question-card with five answer choices, then move the walkthrough to an analysis section below it. Add "Try this before reading the analysis below."
- Add a brief callout before the walkthrough: "Apply the four steps yourself before reading the breakdown."

### Lesson 10-2: The 4-Step Method (approx. 40 blocks)

**Role:** The complete repeatable method for solving any SA question.

**Strengths:**
- Clear process block covering all four steps at the top -- the overview sets the right frame.
- Timing callout (1:45 per question, 2 minutes for conditional questions) is specific and actionable. The step-level time estimates (Steps 1--3 = 60 seconds, Step 4 = 45 seconds) are more useful than a single total.
- The conditional diagramming table (All A are B / If A then B / A only if B / Only A are B / etc.) is accurate, well-formatted, and covers the translation errors that cause the most SA mistakes.
- The "When to Diagram" callout (60--70% of SA questions benefit from diagramming) gives students a calibrated rule of thumb.
- The Prephrase Examples by Gap Type table is strong: linking gap type to template language is highly actionable.
- The Common Wrong Answer Patterns table with three columns (Trap Type / What It Does / How to Spot It) is excellent for recognition training. The Mistaken Reversal is correctly identified as the #1 trap.

**Issues:**
- **The Full Worked Example has no question-card.** The restaurant critic argument (Girard / Café Lumière) is a well-chosen illustrative example, but the four-step walkthrough is presented as a process block with no interactive component. There are no answer choices, and there is no point at which the student can attempt the question before reading the analysis.
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The walkthrough follows the blockquote immediately.
- **The Prephrase section has a typo.** "Prphrase with precision" in the Key Takeaways callout is missing a letter ("Prephrase").
- **The worked example ends in an incomplete state.** Step 4 says "scan the choices for a statement that closes either the 'critic' gap or the 'financial interest' gap" but no answer choices are shown and no resolution is given. The final callout ("Final Check") explains which gap matters most but never reveals what the correct answer would look like. This is a pedagogical dead end.

**Suggested edits:**
- The most impactful fix: present the Girard/Café Lumière argument as a full question-card with five answer choices, then put the current Step-by-Step Walkthrough below it. Add the standard "Try this before reading the analysis" prompt.
- Fix the typo in Key Takeaways ("Prphrase" → "Prephrase").
- Either show the correct answer in Step 4 or add a fifth step to the walkthrough that completes the analysis.

### Lesson 10-3: The Concept Link (PT-120-S-1-Q-12) (approx. 25 blocks)

**Role:** Drill lesson introducing the Concept Link pattern with a real PT question.

**Strengths:**
- The "rogue term" framing is clear and memorable: find the word in the conclusion that does not appear in the premises.
- The "Matching Game" callout is well-placed and captures the core mental task.
- The breakdown analysis of all five answer choices is thorough. Every wrong answer gets a distinct, accurate explanation of why it fails.

**Issues:**

- **The "How to Spot a Concept Link Gap" checklist** (3 bullet points) is useful but appears before the question. Consider whether the concept introduction is long enough to constitute orientation or whether students could jump directly to the question after reading one paragraph.

**Suggested edits:**

- This is the only meaningful gap in this lesson. The content itself is strong.

### Lesson 10-4: The Exclusivity Rule (PT-122-S-2-Q-25) (approx. 28 blocks)

**Role:** Drill lesson on the Exclusivity Rule pattern.

**Strengths:**
- The scope mismatch framing (narrow premises, broad conclusion) is pedagogically clean.
- The "Scope Mismatch" callout explains the core issue precisely: a limited list is being treated as exhaustive.
- The three-row table (Premise Structure / Conclusion Structure / Gap) efficiently maps the pattern.
- The Cecile question (PT-122-S-2-Q-25) is an ideal teaching example because the scope gap is explicit and unambiguous.

- **No "try it first" prompt.** The question-card is followed directly by the Full Analysis section.
- **Answer (D) analysis** notes the timber company is owned by a petrochemical company and calls this a "weakener." This is accurate but deserves a stronger warning: this answer type (one that goes in the opposite direction) should trigger immediate alarm in test-takers.

- Minor: the statement that Answer D is a "weakener" could be phrased as a callout tip: "When an answer choice actively supports the conclusion you are trying to disprove, that is a red flag -- you may have misread the question type."

### Lesson 10-5: The Binary Assumption (PT-123-S-2-Q-23) (approx. 30 blocks)

**Role:** Drill lesson on the Binary Assumption pattern with a logic-dense philosophical argument.

**Strengths:**
- The "hidden third category" framing is the exact right mental model for this pattern.
- Binary vs. Mutually Exclusive is flagged early as a related but distinct pattern, which is excellent forward-referencing.
- The formal logical breakdown of the philosopher's argument (biconditional, IWB → MR, etc.) is rigorous and correct.
- The "Logical Chain" process block clearly shows where the chain gets us (→ ~MW) and where the conclusion needs us to go (→ MR), making the gap visible.
- The "Diagramming Reveals the Gap" tip callout is well-placed.
- The visual table "The Binary Structure Visualized" (With Answer C / Without Answer C) is outstanding -- it shows the transformation from "logical limbo" to logical certainty.

**Issues:**
- **No "try it first" prompt.** The question-card is followed directly by the Full Analysis.
- The "how dense this argument is" is not flagged for students. A student encountering a biconditional philosophical argument for the first time may be overwhelmed. A brief orientation ("this argument is unusually formal -- diagram it carefully before reading the analysis") would help.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a "try it first" prompt. Also consider adding a brief orientation before the Full Analysis: "Note that this stimulus contains a biconditional ('if and only if'). Diagram all three definitions before evaluating the answer choices."

## Structural Issues

### 1. Uniform Absence of "Try It First" Prompts

Every drill lesson (10-3 through 10-11) presents the question-card and immediately follows it with the Full Analysis section. There is no pause, no invitation to attempt the question, and no signal that the analysis is coming. Students who read linearly will see the question and analysis as a single unit rather than as a practice-then-review sequence.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief "try it first" prompt between every question-card and every Full Analysis header in lessons 10-3 through 10-11. This is the module's single highest-priority improvement.

### 2. Introduction and Method Lessons Lack Interactive Practice

Lessons 10-1 and 10-2 contain worked examples but present them entirely as text walkthrough, without question-cards. A student can read the entire introduction and method lesson without ever attempting an SA question independently.

**Recommendation:** Convert the Sandra example in 10-1 and the Girard/Café Lumière example in 10-2 into full question-cards with five answer choices. Both arguments are already structured for this conversion.

### 3. Drill Lessons Are Structurally Consistent (Strength)

Lessons 10-3 through 10-11 all follow the same pattern: pattern introduction, spotting checklist, question-card, step-by-step analysis, wrong answer breakdown, key takeaway. This consistency is a major pedagogical strength -- students can predict the structure and focus on content rather than navigation.
