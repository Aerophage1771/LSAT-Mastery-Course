# Module 24: Main Idea -- Website Content Improvement Report

**Scope:** The 4 website lessons (24-1 through 24-4) evaluated as a standalone course unit.
**Date:** 2026-04-01

---

## Executive Summary

Module 24 covers Main Idea / Main Conclusion questions across four compact lessons: Introduction (24-1), Step-by-Step Approach (24-2), Pattern Recognition (24-3), and Trap Answer Types (24-4). The lessons are well-organized and internally consistent, with each covering a distinct teaching goal. The primary improvement opportunities are:

1. **No practice questions in any lesson.** All four lessons are pure lecture with zero question-cards. The student reads the entire module without attempting a single Main Idea question. This is the module's biggest gap.
2. **Lesson 24-2 is too thin.** At only 10 content blocks, the Step-by-Step Approach lesson covers the 3-step method but lacks a worked example showing the method applied to a real passage. The method is described abstractly with no concrete illustration.
3. **Lesson 24-3 identifies only three patterns.** RC passages can follow many more structural templates. The three listed (Problem/Solution, Correcting the Record, Balanced Summary) are important but not exhaustive. A fourth or fifth pattern would strengthen coverage.
4. **No "try it yourself" prompt exists anywhere.** Even if question-cards are not yet available, a brief prompt before a hypothetical worked analysis would prime active learning.
5. **Lesson 24-1 has mild redundancy in stem identification.** The introductory paragraph about stems partially restates the opening "What is this question asking?" section.

---

## Lesson-by-Lesson Review

### Lesson 24-1: Introduction (10 blocks)

**Role:** Module introduction, stem identification, and importance statement.

**Strengths:**
- Clear opening definition of Main Idea questions with a concise one-sentence goal statement.
- The synthesizing list (problem/solution, common theory/alteration, explanation, comparison) gives students a concrete framework for what correct answers look like.
- Stem Identification breakdown with two categories (Direct Phrasing, Summary & Conclusion Phrasing) covers the real variety of stems accurately.
- The Overarching Takeaway callout effectively unifies the stem variations into a single mental model: find the "umbrella" statement.
- The importance section provides a useful frequency estimate (~11% of RC questions) and correctly notes that main-idea mastery helps with other question types.

**Issues:**
- **Mild redundancy.** The opening paragraphs and the Stem Identification section both explain that Main Idea questions ask for the central idea. The opening could be shorter since the stem section covers the same ground more precisely.
- **No practice question.** The lesson is all definition and identification with no opportunity for the student to try anything.
- **Missing common stem variants.** Some stems ask about the "primary purpose" of the passage, which overlaps with Main Idea. A note distinguishing or connecting these would be helpful.

**Suggested edits:**
- Tighten the opening to 1-2 sentences; let the stem identification breakdown carry the definitional weight.
- Add a mini-exercise: "Read this 3-sentence passage and identify which of the following best states the main point."
- Add a note about how Main Idea stems relate to (or differ from) Primary Purpose stems.

---

### Lesson 24-2: Step-by-Step Approach (10 blocks)

**Role:** The complete method for solving Main Idea questions.

**Strengths:**
- Clear 3-step process block: Read for the Overall Argument, Evaluate Using a Two-Step Test, Confirm.
- The Two-Step Test (Accuracy Test + Comprehensiveness Test) is the lesson's strongest teaching moment -- it gives students a concrete elimination framework.
- The bullet list pointing students to key locations (last sentence of first paragraph, first sentence of each body paragraph, final paragraph) is practical and actionable.

**Issues:**
- **Too thin.** At 10 content blocks, this is the shortest method lesson in any module. The 3-step process is described but never demonstrated. There is no worked example, no sample passage, and no question-card.
- **No illustration of the Two-Step Test.** The Accuracy Test and Comprehensiveness Test are explained abstractly but not shown in action. A before/after example -- "Choice A passes the Accuracy Test but fails the Comprehensiveness Test because..." -- would make the method concrete.
- **Step 1 advice is generic.** "Read for the Overall Argument and Predict the Main Idea" is sound but could be more specific. What exactly should the student write down? What does a good prediction look like?

**Suggested edits:**
- Add a worked example demonstrating all three steps on a sample passage or a real PT passage.
- Show the Two-Step Test in action: demonstrate one answer choice passing both tests and one failing the Comprehensiveness Test.
- Provide an example of a strong prediction vs. a weak prediction.

---

### Lesson 24-3: Pattern Recognition (5 blocks)

**Role:** Teaches students to recognize common passage structures and predict correct answer forms.

**Strengths:**
- Three pattern types (Problem/Solution/Critique, Correcting the Record/Debate, Balanced Summary) are well-chosen and cover the most common RC structures.
- Each pattern includes both an identification strategy (clues to look for) and a correct-answer description (what the right answer looks like). This dual approach is pedagogically strong.
- Badge labels (Narrative, Contrast, Synthesis) provide quick mental handles for each pattern.

**Issues:**
- **Only three patterns.** This is a very short lesson (5 content blocks including the heading). Common RC structures like "Historical Development" (tracing an evolution over time) or "Case Study" (examining one specific example in depth) are absent.
- **No examples.** The patterns are described abstractly without any concrete passage excerpts illustrating each one.
- **No practice question.** After learning three patterns, the student has no chance to identify which pattern a given passage follows.

**Suggested edits:**
- Add 1-2 more pattern types (e.g., Historical Development, Case Study/Deep Dive).
- Add a brief concrete example for at least one pattern -- even a 2-3 sentence mock passage illustrating the Problem/Solution structure.
- Add a mini-exercise: "Which pattern does this passage follow?"

---

### Lesson 24-4: Trap Answer Types (5 blocks)

**Role:** Teaches students to recognize four common wrong-answer patterns.

**Strengths:**
- Four trap types (Inaccurate Information, Outside the Scope/Too Extreme, Too Narrow, Opposing Viewpoint) are well-chosen and cover the real wrong-answer landscape.
- The "Too Narrow" trap is correctly identified as the most frequent distractor -- this is accurate and valuable prioritization.
- Each trap includes a clear "Flaw" statement summarizing why it fails.
- Badges (Factual Error, Unsupported, Partial Truth, Wrong Side) provide quick identification handles.

**Issues:**
- **No examples.** Each trap is defined but never illustrated with a concrete wrong-answer example. Even one hypothetical example per trap would strengthen retention.
- **No practice question.** The lesson teaches four traps but never asks the student to identify which trap a given wrong answer represents.
- **The "Inaccurate Information" trap description mentions "the first half is correct, but the second half slips in an error."** This is a useful insight but could be expanded with a structural pattern (e.g., "Correct subject + incorrect predicate").

**Suggested edits:**
- Add one concrete example for each trap type.
- Add a mini-exercise: "Which trap does each wrong answer represent?"
- Expand the "first half correct / second half wrong" observation into a general detection heuristic.

## Structural Issues

### 1. No Practice Questions in the Entire Module

The module's most significant gap is the complete absence of question-cards. Across all four lessons and ~30 content blocks, a student encounters zero practice questions. Every lesson is pure theory. This is especially problematic for a question type that accounts for ~11% of RC questions and is described as "critical" in the introduction.

**Recommendation:** Add at least one question-card to Lesson 24-2 (demonstrating the method) and one to Lesson 24-4 (identifying traps in real wrong answers). Ideally, every lesson would have at least a lightweight exercise.

### 2. Lesson 24-2 Lacks a Worked Example

The Step-by-Step Approach lesson describes the method but never shows it in action. Without a worked example, students must trust the abstract description and figure out the application on their own.

**Recommendation:** Add a full worked example with a passage excerpt, the 3-step method applied, and the Two-Step Test demonstrated on at least two answer choices.

### 3. Pattern and Trap Lessons Are Too Thin

Lessons 24-3 and 24-4 have only 5 content blocks each. While brevity can be a strength, these lessons lack the concrete examples and practice exercises needed to make the abstract concepts stick.

**Recommendation:** Expand each with 2-3 concrete examples and one mini-exercise.

## Summary of Priorities

1. **Add practice questions to the module** -- the complete absence of question-cards is the biggest gap
2. **Add a worked example to Lesson 24-2** -- the method lesson needs to show, not just tell
3. **Expand Lessons 24-3 and 24-4 with concrete examples** -- abstract trap and pattern definitions need illustrations
4. **Add "try it first" prompts before any future worked analyses** -- small change, big pedagogical impact
5. **Add 1-2 more passage patterns to Lesson 24-3** -- three patterns is too few for comprehensive coverage
